Logic framework

Square of Opposition

The Square of Opposition is a visual tool used in logic to diagram the four categorical propositions.

A: Universal affirmative
E: Universal negative
I: Particular affirmative
O: Particular negative

The diagonal corners represent contradictions: they both cannot be true and they both cannot be false. Contradictions differ in quality (is/is not) and quantity (all/some).

The top two (A and E) are called contraries. Contraries cannot both be true but they can both be false.

The bottom two (I and O) are called sub-contraries. They cannot both be false but they can both be true.

The sides give us the sub-alternation relationship. If the top is true (A or E), the bottom on the same side is also true: if A is true then I is true; if E is true then O is true.

A common mistake is to read “some” as “only some.” The “some” of the I proposition can be paired with the universal A because if “all” is true, then it also follows that “some” is true. If all apples are fruit, then some apples — the ones at the store — are also fruit. This is the basis for deduction.

Visual note: the gold bracket under the two lower corners marks the theistic synthesis: some is eternal and some is not eternal together express the claim that God alone is eternal, while creation is not.

None Is Eternal
Nothing is eternal.

Nothing is eternal means the same as all is temporal.

And all is temporal means the same as all had a beginning.

And all had a beginning means the same as all came into being.

And if all came into being, then it did not come from another being because all came into being.

Therefore, all came into being means the same as all came into being from non-being.

But being and non-being are fundamentally different. A chicken can come from an egg because, while chickens are not eggs, they are both beings.

If a being can come from either being or non-being, then on that point being and non-being are not different. Being is non-being. That is a contradiction.

Therefore, it cannot be true that all being came into being from non-being.

And if that is false, then falsehood traces all the way back up to “none is eternal.” It cannot be true that none is eternal.

And if “none is eternal” cannot be true, then something must be eternal.

We are left with this question: is everything eternal, or are only some things eternal?

Substance

What Is Substance?

Philosopher reference: Aristotle gives the classic account of substance and accidents; Aquinas develops created substance as dependent being; Descartes, Spinoza, Leibniz, and Locke give important modern alternatives.

Substance is one step up from being. Being is what exists. Substance is the kind of being that exists in which properties adhere and activities describe.

For instance, the property of "being red" or the activities of "running" or "thinking" do not exist in themselves. Something is red, and something is running or thinking.

There are two possible substances: matter and spirit (mind/soul/self). Matter is extended (takes up space/can be physically measured) and non-conscious; spirit is non-extended and conscious.

Each view about what is eternal must make a claim about substance. The monist views say there is only one substance and they reduce everything else to that one substance.

The materialist says all is matter and thinking is done by the brain. The idealist says all is mind and material things only appear to exist.

Dualists say both matter and spirit exist and both have existed from eternity.

Theists say both matter and spirits exist, but only God (who is a spirit) has existed from eternity and he brought matter and finite spirits into existence.

Argument 1

Not All Spirit Is Eternal

To show that not all spirit is eternal also shows that reincarnation is not true.

Step 1: If all spirit, mind, or soul is eternal, then my soul is eternal.

Step 2: If my soul is eternal — if I existed before my body and without any beginning — then I would have infinite knowledge, or all knowledge.

Step 3: I do not have infinite knowledge.

Conclusion: Therefore, I have not existed from eternity, and at least one soul — mine — is not eternal.

Whatever spirit is eternal must also be infinite in knowledge. Since I am not infinite in knowledge, my soul is not eternal. This means Spiritual Monism cannot be true if it claims that all spirit or soul is eternal.

Option 1

Is the Cause in My Mind?

If the cause of what I see were in my mind, I would have total control over it, as I do with anything I imagine in my mind.

I do not have control over what I see in the same way that I have control over what I imagine in my mind.

Therefore, what I see is not caused by something merely in my mind.

Option 2

Is the Cause in Another’s Mind?

If the cause of what I see were in another’s mind, then I would have no control over it, any more than I have control over what another person imagines in his mind.

But I do have some control over what I see, unlike what another person imagines in his mind.

Therefore, the cause of what I see is not in another’s mind.

Option 3

The Cause Is Outside of All Minds

If the cause of what I see is not in my mind or another’s mind, then it is outside of all minds.

This is what we mean by a material being: something extended, taking up space, and existing outside of minds.

Therefore, some material being exists, and Spiritual Monism is false.

Advaita Vedanta

Arguments Against Advaita Vedanta

Advaita Vedanta teaches that all is One without any parts. It says that the world is an illusion or dream in the mind of Brahman.

1) The problem of divine ignorance

If God is all-knowing, how can there be an illusion about which God is ignorant or misled?

If Brahman alone exists and Brahman is without error, then illusion is difficult to explain. An all-knowing God cannot be deceived, mistaken, or under an illusion.

2) The problem of the cause of the dream

What is the cause of the dream?

It cannot be God, because God is all-knowing and without error. But it also cannot be anything else, because on this view only God exists.

3) Is the illusion eternal or temporal?

If the illusion is eternal, then it will always exist, and there is no purpose in striving to reach enlightenment.

If the illusion is temporal, then why did God change from not being under an illusion to being under an illusion?

4) The problem of self-forgetting

If I am God, how did I change from knowing that I am God to not knowing that I am God, if the only thing that exists is God?

This creates a contradiction: the one reality is said to be all-knowing, yet somehow becomes ignorant of itself.

Dvaita Option 1

All Parts Are Finite

If all the parts are the same and they are all finite, then they cannot add up to an infinite whole.

But God is infinite.

Therefore, God is not made up of finite parts.

Additionally, whatever is finite cannot have existed from eternity. If it had existed from eternity, it would have infinite knowledge already.

How many reincarnations do you need to learn that you are God? If you have existed from eternity, you would already know this.

You do not know it.

Therefore, you have not existed from eternity.

Dvaita Option 2

All Parts Are Infinite

If all the parts are the same and they are all infinite, then we have a contradiction.

What is infinite is not the part of something else. “Infinite” means that than which there is no greater, and “part” means that which is less than the whole.

An “infinite part” is therefore a contradiction.

Dvaita Option 3

Some Parts Are Infinite and Some Are Finite

If some parts are infinite and some are finite, then we have the same contradiction. The “infinite part” cannot be part of something larger that is made when you add the finite parts.

And the finite parts cannot have existed from eternity.

If the reply is that the infinite part brought the finite parts into existence, then this is no longer “All is One.” It is instead describing creation.

The infinite part created the finite parts. That is theism.

The Scholastics, such as Thomas Aquinas, taught that Reason led philosophers like Aristotle to deny God the Creator and to teach that matter is eternally dependent on God. If we use Reason to consider the claim “eternally dependent,” we see that it leads to a contradiction. There can be nothing that is eternally dependent.

Time

What Is Time?

Time is the measurement of change. If there is nothing changing, there is no change to measure and therefore no time.

Time measures the change in material beings and in finite souls, which think one thought after another.

Time does not apply to God, who is unchanging.

Time began when God brought material beings and finite souls into existence. Time is not without beginning because neither matter nor finite souls are without beginning.

None is eternal

The view 'None is eternal' claims there was once nothing at all and then there was something.

Key distinction: This view is not teaching that each individual thing had a beginning but there was a beginningless line of such things. It is saying that there was once only nothing.

On this view, nothing is eternal. Not matter. Not spirit. Not a divine cause. Reality as a whole begins. The universe is not an everlasting line of changing material things, nor an eternal spiritual order, nor a duality of matter and spirit. Instead, there is first nothing at all, and only later something.

That is why this position must be distinguished from material monism. Material monism can say that each individual thing has a beginning while the total material order never began. “None is eternal” denies even that. It says there is no eternal line behind present things.

Conceptual note

This is included here as a distinct logical possibility in the map. It is much more radical than the ordinary ancient materialist claim of an eternal material order.

Contrast with Material Monism

Material monism says the whole line of material reality is eternal even if particular forms are not. This branch says the whole line itself had a beginning from nothing.

Contrast with Spiritual Monism

There could be forms of spiritual monism, like Buddhism, that say no individual soul or event is eternal but there is no beginning of this line.

Argument

The Argument Against None is Eternal

When we use Reason to analyze the proposition “None is Eternal,” we find that it leads to a contradiction. Therefore, it cannot be true. If “None is Eternal” is false, then its contradictory—“Some is Eternal”—must be true. Something must have existed from eternity. The task of metaphysics is to determine what that is.

Step 1: “None is Eternal” is equivalent to “All being is temporal” by obversion.

Step 2: “All being is temporal” means “All being had a beginning.”

Step 3: “All being had a beginning” means “All being came into being.”

Step 4: “All being came into being” implies that all being came into being from non-being.

This leads to a contradiction. Being and non-being are fundamentally different and have nothing in common. But if being can come from non-being, then on that point being and non-being would be the same. That is the contradiction.

If being cannot come from non-being, then the claim that all being came from non-being cannot be true. Therefore, all being did not have a beginning, and not all being is temporal.

Therefore, the original claim “None is Eternal” cannot be true.

Conclusion: Since “None is Eternal” is false, its contradictory must be true: “Some is Eternal.” Something must have existed from eternity.

These questions often arise once we say that God is eternal, unchanging, and the Creator of all things.

When did God create?

Minor Objection

When Did God Create?

There is no before creation. Creation is the beginning of time, and “before” is a time word. Therefore, this question is a contradiction, like asking, “What was God doing in time when there was no time?”

God is the Creator of time by creating beings that change.

God is not dependent on waiting for the future. Nor does God merely know the future by looking ahead, as if he skips to the end of a book to see what happens. God knows the future because he created it: he wrote the book.

Minor Objection

Why Did God Create?

God is infinite and has no needs. Creation does not fill a need in God, such as a need for relationships, energy, or attention.

To be is to do. Whatever God does is a revelation of who God is. If God did not create, that too would be a revelation of who God is.

By creating, God reveals his glory, perfection, and excellence.

Minor Objection

How Did God Create?

Creation does not deplete God. Creation is not a part of God. God brings new things into existence.

This is not the same as being from non-being or an uncaused event, because God is the cause.

Two analogies

The creation of matter is analogous to your non-physical intention to move your arm becoming the motion of your arm.

The creation of other minds is analogous to parents having a baby: that baby’s mind is not made from parts of the parents’ minds.

Minor Objection

Can an Eternal God Act in History?

We already know Open Theism, which says God learns the future as it happens, is false because God is unchanging and knows the future as the Creator. But some object that this makes God distant and unable to interact with creation.

Divine Action

Does Interaction Change God?

An interaction in history, such as parting the Red Sea when Moses is there, is not a change in God.

From time’s perspective, we say that is now a past-tense event. But that is not a change in God.

Statements like “God regretted making man” are temporal revelations of eternal truths about God. God always hates sin, but this comes to expression in specific historical events.

Divine Action

Why Did God Not Act Sooner?

God did not act to part the Red Sea sooner or regret making man sooner because there is a logical order to these events.

God must part the Red Sea when Moses is there, not before or after.

This temporal sequence is an eternal logical sequence.

Minor Objection

How Is God Known?

God is known as he reveals himself. There is no way to know God except as he reveals himself.

General revelation

Creation is called general revelation. General revelation is what all persons at all times can and should know about God.

Special revelation

Scripture is called special revelation. Special revelation is redemptive revelation given by God to prophets and written down for preservation.

It communicates how God will redeem mankind, who is lost in sin, and how God will be both just, because sin requires a payment, and merciful, because sin is forgiven.

We can narrow down which Scripture is from God by looking for the Scripture that faithfully teaches the true definition of God and is about redemption.

Major Objection

The Problem of Evil

The major objection to theism is the one you find behind every other objection. An atheist might start with a minor objection, but when you answer that, he usually backs up to this major objection: the problem of evil.

If God is all good, all knowing, and all powerful, then why is there evil? A solution to this problem must keep intact that God is indeed all good, all knowing, and all powerful, and that while evil now exists, it does not present a contradiction with who God is.

Aristotle’s Four Causes

To understand what a thing is, we must explain why it is. Aristotle’s four causes are four kinds of explanation.

Material Cause

What it is made of. A statue is made of bronze; a table is made of wood; a body is made of material parts.

Formal Cause

What it is: the form, essence, or nature that makes a thing the kind of thing it is.

Efficient Cause

What brought it into being or produced the change. The sculptor is the efficient cause of the statue.

Final Cause

The end, purpose, or good for which the thing exists or acts.

Key insight: Modern thought often keeps efficient cause while rejecting final cause. That shift changes ontology because beings are no longer understood in relation to their ends.

Core Ontology Concept

What Is a Substance?

A substance is a being that exists in itself rather than in another. Ontology begins by distinguishing the thing itself from the properties that belong to it.

Substance

A substance is what has being in itself. A tree, a horse, and a person are substances. They are not merely qualities floating by themselves; they are the things that have qualities.

Accidents or properties

Properties exist in or of substances. Greenness, height, shape, location, posture, and motion do not normally exist by themselves. They exist as features of some substance.

Tree and greenness

The tree is the substance. Greenness is a property of the tree. If the leaves change color, the tree has changed, but the tree has not therefore ceased to be a tree.

Person and thoughts

A person is not merely a bundle of thoughts. The person is the thinking substance, and thoughts are acts or states of that person.

Why this matters

Without the distinction between substance and properties, the categories float without a subject, dualism is harder to understand, and the mind-body problem becomes confused.

Core Ontology Concept
Identity Over Time

If something changes, how is it still the same thing? The problem of identity over time asks what remains the same through change.

The question

If a thing gains and loses properties, moves from place to place, grows, decays, learns, or forgets, what makes it the same thing across those changes?

Ship of Theseus

If every plank of a ship is gradually replaced, is it still the same ship? The example presses the difference between material parts and the form, order, function, and continuity of a thing.

Personal identity

A person changes in body, memory, knowledge, habits, and desires. Yet we still say the same person persists. This means the person cannot be reduced to one passing property or one momentary mental state.

Substance and identity

Identity over time requires a subject of change. Something must undergo the change. Otherwise, there are only disconnected moments with no being that changes.

Why this matters

This connects metaphysics to self-knowledge. If you cannot explain what you are through change, then you do not yet know yourself.

Medieval Ontology
The 5 Ways of Thomas Aquinas

Thomas Aquinas presents the Five Ways in the Summa Theologiae as arguments from features of the world to a first explanatory source. Each way moves from something evident in created reality to a conclusion about a first principle, and then Aquinas says that this is what all men call God.

1. The Argument from Motion

Things in the world move or change. Whatever is moved is moved by another, and an infinite regress of movers would not explain present motion. Therefore, there must be a first unmoved mover, “and this all men call God.”

2. The Argument from Efficient Cause

We observe an order of efficient causes. Nothing is the efficient cause of itself, and an infinite regress of causes would leave the present order unexplained. Therefore, there must be a first efficient cause, “to which everyone gives the name of God.”

3. The Argument from Possibility and Necessity

Things in the world are contingent: they can exist or not exist. If everything were merely possible or contingent, then at some time nothing would have existed, and nothing would exist now. Therefore, there must be a necessary being whose necessity is not received from another, “and this all men speak of as God.”

4. The Argument from Degrees of Perfection

We recognize degrees of goodness, truth, nobility, and other perfections. Degrees imply some maximum or standard by which lesser degrees are measured. Therefore, there must be something that is the maximum in being, goodness, and perfection, “and this we call God.”

5. The Argument from Governance or Finality

Natural things that lack knowledge nevertheless act toward ends. Since they do not direct themselves knowingly, their ordered action must be directed by an intelligent being. Therefore, there is an intelligent governor of nature, “and this being we call God.”

Critique: Even from a theistic perspective these arguments can be critiqued. First, they are examples of overextension. What the argument proves is far less than the conclusion “This is God.” None of them alone or together proves that God is a spirit, infinite, eternal, and unchanging, in being, wisdom, power, holiness, justice, goodness, and truth. Second, they are often misleading to current readers. Aquinas, following Aristotle, believed that God could be the creator of the world and yet the world has no beginning. Aquinas did not think that reason can prove the world had a beginning, and in fact thought that the preponderance of arguments led to thinking the world has existed from eternity and we can only know otherwise by faith in the Bible. These five ways will be challenged in modern ontology for assuming that reality reflects human arguments: how do we know that reason is ontological (applies to both thinking and being)?

Contemporary Ontology
Process and Dialectic

This page studies a family of views that explain reality, history, nature, and society primarily through process, conflict, negation, and development. Instead of beginning with eternal being, fixed natures, and stable forms, dialectical and process philosophies often begin with becoming: reality is said to unfold through opposition, transformation, and historical movement.

Basic idea:

A dialectical view explains development through tension or contradiction. One state of affairs gives rise to its opposite, and the conflict between them produces a new stage. A process view similarly treats becoming, change, event, or historical development as more basic than permanent substance.

1. Hegel: dialectic as the development of thought and history

In Hegel, reality is not best understood as a collection of fixed, isolated things. It is understood as a rational historical process. Ideas, institutions, and forms of consciousness develop through conflict and negation. A position reveals an internal tension; that tension produces opposition; and through the struggle a higher stage emerges. This is often summarized as thesis, antithesis, and synthesis, though that exact formula is a simplified classroom shorthand rather than Hegel’s own preferred terminology.

For this map, the key point is that Hegel shifts attention from stable being to historical becoming. Truth is treated as something that unfolds in a process. The real is understood through development.

2. Marx: dialectic turned toward material history

Marx takes the dialectical pattern and gives it a material and economic form. Where Hegel’s dialectic emphasizes consciousness, reason, and spirit, Marx emphasizes material conditions, class conflict, labor, property, and economic power. History is interpreted as a struggle between opposed social classes, and each social order contains internal contradictions that eventually produce a new order.

In Marx, feudalism gives way to capitalism, capitalism produces the conflict between bourgeoisie and proletariat, and that conflict is supposed to lead toward socialism and communism. The dialectic becomes a theory of social revolution.

3. Darwin: dialectical biology

Darwin’s theory of evolution is not Hegelian in the strict historical sense, but it can be understood as a form of dialectical biology because it explains living forms through struggle, opposition, adaptation, and transformation over time. Organisms face environmental pressures, competition, scarcity, and death. Variations that survive are preserved, while others are eliminated. Species are not treated as fixed forms but as populations that change through conflict with conditions of life.

In this sense, Darwinian evolution gives biology a process-centered account of life. Stable essences are replaced by historical development; biological order is explained through selection, struggle, and gradual transformation.

4. Process theology: God and the world in mutual development

Process theology applies a process metaphysic to the doctrine of God. Instead of affirming God as eternal, immutable, simple, and fully actual, process theology often presents God as related to the world in such a way that God and the world are involved in an ongoing history of becoming. God is not the unmoved mover who creates all things from nothing and governs them by sovereign providence. God is reconceived as one who persuades, responds, suffers, and develops with the world.

From the standpoint of classical Christian metaphysics, this is a serious departure because it makes change more ultimate than divine immutability and makes God’s relation to creation part of a larger process.

5. Liberation theology: dialectic applied to oppression and deliverance

Liberation theology applies a conflict model to theology and society. It often interprets history through the opposition between oppressor and oppressed and then reads Christian doctrine through that social struggle. Sin is emphasized as structural injustice; salvation is often described in terms of liberation from oppressive political, economic, and social arrangements.

At its best, liberation theology draws attention to real suffering and injustice. Its danger is that the biblical categories of sin, redemption, atonement, repentance, and reconciliation can be subordinated to a dialectical political framework.

6. Social justice and cultural Marxism: dialectic applied to identity groups

Modern social justice theories often use a dialectical structure even when they do not explicitly name Hegel or Marx. Society is divided into oppressor and oppressed groups. Moral authority is assigned by social location. Institutions, language, customs, and inherited norms are analyzed as instruments of domination. The goal becomes not merely equal treatment under a shared moral law, but the transformation of society by exposing and overturning hidden systems of power.

The phrase cultural Marxism is used in different ways. In this map, it names the extension of Marx’s conflict pattern from economics to culture: class conflict becomes race conflict, sex conflict, gender conflict, sexuality conflict, colonizer/colonized conflict, or privileged/marginalized conflict. The dialectic is moved from the factory into the classroom, family, church, law, literature, and language.

7. Critical Theory and the Frankfurt School

The Frankfurt School developed a form of critical social theory that combined Marx, Freud, Hegel, and modern cultural analysis. Rather than focusing only on economic class, it examined culture, authority, family, religion, mass media, and social psychology. The point of theory was not merely to describe society but to criticize and transform it.

Critical Theory therefore keeps the dialectical impulse: it looks for contradictions, hidden domination, alienation, repression, and structures of power. Later critical theories apply this method to race, gender, sexuality, colonialism, disability, and other social categories.

Metaphysical concern:

The dialectical imagination can notice real change, conflict, and historical development. But if process and conflict become ultimate, then permanent being, fixed natures, moral law, creation order, and divine immutability are displaced. The central metaphysical question remains: is reality finally grounded in eternal being, or is reality finally a process of becoming?

Arguments

Arguments Against Material Monism

Material monism makes two claims: 1) all is matter, and 2) matter is eternal. We can use arguments to refute both of these claims.

1) Matter is not eternal because it is changing toward a final condition

Material monism claims that matter has existed from eternity. To evaluate this, we use Reason to compare the ideas of what it means to be eternal and what it means to change.

An essential feature of matter is that it is changing. Matter is always in motion, undergoing transformation, decay, and development. An essential feature of what is eternal, however, is that it is unchanging, or else that any change is part of a perfectly balanced eternal cycle.

But matter does not exhibit this kind of eternal cycle. Instead, it changes in a directed way toward a final condition. For example, materialists themselves describe the “heat death” of the universe, a state in which all energy is evenly distributed and no further change is possible.

This is not only true of the universe as a whole, but also of its parts: the sun, stars, galaxies, and other physical systems move toward exhaustion or equilibrium.

If matter had existed from eternity and has always been changing toward such a final condition, then it would have already reached that final condition. But it has not.

Therefore, matter has not been changing from eternity. And if matter has not been changing from eternity, then matter itself has not existed from eternity. Matter had a beginning.

2) Not all is matter: the soul is not identical to the brain

Material monism claims that all that exists is matter. In particular, it claims that the soul or mind is identical to the brain. To evaluate this, we again use Reason by comparing the properties of each to see if they are identical.

The soul thinks. A thought is the kind of thing that can be true or false. For example, the thought “2 + 2 = 4” is true, while “2 + 2 = 5” is false. Truth and falsity are essential properties of thoughts.

The brain, however, is a material object composed of neurons. Neurons have spatial and motion properties such as fast or slow, up or down, left or right. These are the kinds of properties that belong to matter.

If the soul were identical to the brain, then their properties would be identical. But they are not. Thinking and motion are not the same.

Therefore, the soul is not identical to the brain. Not all is matter.

The soul is immaterial, non-extended, and conscious. It has causal interaction with the body: it can move the body, and the body can affect the soul. But the soul’s existence is not the same as material existence.

3) From Material Monism to Spiritual Monism

Material monism is sometimes called naive realism. It is like someone who says, “I only believe what I can see.” The materialist thinks he can see matter and therefore is safe believing that matter exists. But because he cannot see a soul or mind, he concludes that the soul does not exist.

However, when we use Reason to analyze what we see, it turns out that we do not directly see matter. We see an image in the mind.

After all, if I am looking at my computer, my computer does not end up in my eyes or in my brain in order to be seen. Nor are there any actual computers inside my brain. But what I see looks like a computer. Therefore, what I see is not simply my eyes or my brain.

This means that the real problem is not proving that the soul exists. We know the soul exists just by thinking about thought itself. The deeper problem is proving that there is a material world existing outside of all minds.

This is why naive realism often gives way to some form of idealism or spiritual monism as the more sophisticated philosophical viewpoint. Once the materialist begins to analyze perception carefully, the certainty he thought he had about matter becomes less obvious than the reality of the mind doing the perceiving.

Free Will and Moral Evil

Free will is having an unhindered will: you can do what you want and are not restricted.

Humans must have free will in order to commit moral evil. Trees do not commit moral evil. But this does not explain why humans used their free will to sin. God could have created a world with free persons who used their free will to love him.

Nor does explaining how humans sin explain why there is sin. Humans sin when they disobey God. Humans disobey God when they put themselves and their will in the place of God. Humans do that when they fail to use Reason to distinguish between God and themselves as creatures.

This is atheism. Moral evil begins in unbelief and the failure to use Reason.

Yet it is still true that God could have created humans who freely used Reason to love him. God created persons who did not use Reason to love him, but instead disobeyed. They are a-theists.

The problem of evil says that God is not free to create atheists. But God is free to create atheists and a world with the moral evil they committed.

But why? That gets us back to the minor objection, “Why did God create?” By creating atheists, God can also reveal the glory of redemption and the excellence of his justice and mercy.

The person using the problem of evil to reject God is saying that God is not free to reveal the glory of redemption, but that is clearly not true.

It is ironic because the atheist is saying: “My existence is proof that God does not exist,” or, “If God exists, he would not make a world with me in it.” He is saying that because he rejects that Reason and Creation reveal God, he does not believe in God.

Advanced Ontology Concept
Universals

A universal is what can be truly said of many particular things. We say that Socrates is human, Plato is human, and Aristotle is human. The question is whether “human” names something real, something in the mind, or merely a word.

The problem

Particulars are individual things: this man, this tree, this triangle. Universals are repeatable intelligible realities: humanity, treeness, triangularity, justice, goodness. Ontology asks what status universals have.

Realism — Plato

Plato held that universals or Forms are real and more stable than changing sensible things. Particulars participate in Forms. This explains why knowledge needs something permanent, but it risks separating universals too sharply from the things we know.

Moderate Realism — Aristotle

Aristotle held that forms are real, but they exist in substances rather than in a separate world. The mind abstracts the universal from particular beings. This preserves both real knowledge and real individual things.

Nominalism — Ockham

Nominalism says universals are not real things outside the mind. They are names, signs, or mental ways of grouping particulars. This avoids multiplying entities, but it threatens to reduce common natures to labels.

Why this matters

If universals are not real in any way, then knowledge, definition, classification, and moral concepts become unstable. If “human nature” is only a word, then claims about what humans are and what is good for humans lose their metaphysical footing.

Advanced Ontology Concept
Essence vs Existence

Essence answers the question, What is it? Existence answers the question, Is it? This distinction becomes central in medieval metaphysics because it helps explain the difference between God and creatures.

Philosopher reference: Avicenna helps sharpen the essence/existence distinction; Aquinas uses it to explain creaturely dependence and divine self-existence.
Essence

The essence of a thing is what it is. The essence of a triangle is a three-sided plane figure. The essence of a human includes rational animality. A mind can understand an essence even when the thing does not presently exist.

Existence

Existence is that a thing is. Knowing what a phoenix is does not prove that a phoenix exists. In creatures, essence and existence are distinct: what a creature is does not explain that it is.

Creatures

Creatures receive existence. They are not self-explanatory. A tree, a star, an angel, or a human soul does not contain the sufficient reason for its own existence in its essence.

God

God is not one more being whose essence is distinct from existence. God is self-existent being. God does not receive existence; God is infinite, eternal, and unchangeable being.

Why this matters

If created things have essence distinct from existence, then created things are dependent. They cannot be the eternal, independent ground of reality. This pushes metaphysics toward theism.

Advanced Ontology Concept
Causation: Can There Be Uncaused Events?

Causation asks how one being explains the existence, change, motion, or state of another. The central question is not merely whether events follow other events, but whether anything can begin to be without a cause.

Philosopher reference: Aristotle and Aquinas give the classical causal framework. Hume, Kant, Mill, Russell, and later analytic philosophy challenge necessity, while Anscombe helps recover causation as more than mere regular sequence.
Core claim

If an event begins to be without a cause, then it comes into being from non-being. But non-being is not something. It has no power, properties, potential, actuality, or ability to produce. Therefore, uncaused events are unintelligible.

Classical causation

A cause is not merely something that happens before an effect. It is that by which something exists, changes, moves, or comes to be. Aristotle’s tools help us ask what a thing is made of, what it is, what brings it about, and what end it serves.

The metaphysical issue

The issue is deeper than prediction. We are asking whether being can arise from non-being. If being and non-being are genuinely different, then non-being cannot be treated as though it can produce being.

Argument against uncaused events

1. If an event begins without a cause, then it comes into being from non-being.

2. Non-being has no actuality, power, property, or potential.

3. What has no actuality, power, property, or potential cannot produce being.

4. Therefore, no event can come into being from non-being.

5. Therefore, uncaused events are impossible.

Historical Challenge 1
Sextus Empiricus: Skepticism about Causes

Sextus Empiricus represents the skeptical challenge: when philosophers disagree about causes, the skeptic suspends judgment rather than affirming that causes are known.

The challenge

The skeptic argues that we do not get behind appearances to a necessary causal structure. We observe sequences and disputes over explanation, but not a cause with certainty.

The reply

Skepticism about whether we can identify a particular cause does not make an uncaused event intelligible. Doubt about this or that causal account is not a proof that being can come from non-being.

Map conclusion

The skeptic may question our knowledge of causes, but he cannot coherently explain how a new being arises from nothing. Suspension of judgment is not an explanation of being.

Historical Challenge 2
Leibniz and Occasionalism: Dependence Is Not No Cause

Leibniz and the occasionalist tradition raise the relation between created causes and divine action. The important distinction is between dependent causes and uncaused events.

The challenge

If God sustains all things, then created causes are not independent ultimate causes. Occasionalism pushes this further and says God is the true cause of events that creatures seem to cause.

The reply

This does not support uncaused events. It moves causation upward to God. Dependence on God is not the absence of cause; it is the deepest account of cause.

Map conclusion

The question is not whether creatures are independent causes. They are not. The question is whether anything can begin from non-being. A divine cause answers that question; uncaused events do not.

Historical Challenge 3
David Hume: Constant Conjunction and Habit

Hume argues that we do not perceive necessary connection. We see one event followed by another, and the mind forms a habit of expectation.

The challenge

On Hume’s account, causation is not known as a necessary relation in being. It is reduced to regular sequence and psychological expectation.

The reply

Hume shifts from ontology to psychology. The fact that I do not see necessary connection with my eyes does not show that necessary causal dependence is unreal.

Map conclusion

If causation is only habit, explanation collapses into expectation. But metaphysics asks why being exists and changes, not merely what we expect to happen next.

Historical Challenge 4
Immanuel Kant: Causation as a Category of Experience

Kant treats causation as one of the categories by which the mind structures experience. This answers Hume, but it creates a new problem for metaphysics.

The challenge

If causation is a condition of experience rather than a principle known to belong to being, then causation is secured for appearances but not for things as they are in themselves.

The reply

The metaphysical question cannot stop at how we organize experience. We are asking whether being itself can begin without a cause. That is an ontological issue, not merely a structure of consciousness.

Map conclusion

Kant protects scientific experience from Humean skepticism, but he does not finally ground causation as a principle of being. The map must go deeper than the conditions of experience.

Historical Challenge 5
William James: Pragmatism and Practical Consequences

James shifts attention toward practical consequences, lived options, and what difference a belief makes in experience.

The challenge

Pragmatism can treat causal and metaphysical claims in terms of their practical use rather than their necessary relation to being.

The reply

Usefulness does not answer the question of origin. Even if a belief works, metaphysics still asks whether anything can begin from nothing.

Map conclusion

The question of causation is not settled by what helps us navigate experience. It is settled by whether the proposed account makes being intelligible.

Historical Challenge 6
John Stuart Mill: Empirical Regularity

Mill represents an empirical account of causation in which causes are discovered through observation, comparison, and regularity.

The challenge

Empiricism gives methods for identifying patterns in experience, but it struggles to ground causation as a necessary principle of being.

The reply

Repeated observation can show that events are regularly connected, but it does not by itself explain why being cannot arise from non-being.

Map conclusion

Empirical methods are useful for studying created causes. But the metaphysical principle that being cannot come from non-being is more basic than repeated observation.

Historical Challenge 7
Bertrand Russell: “The Universe Is Just There”

Russell’s famous response to the question of the universe’s cause is to say that the universe is simply there. This refuses the demand for an ultimate explanation.

The challenge

The claim “the universe is just there” treats the whole of reality as a brute fact. It does not explain why there is being rather than non-being.

The reply

A refusal to explain is not an explanation. If the universe is temporal, changing, or dependent, then it cannot be the final account of being.

Map conclusion

Brute fact metaphysics stops precisely where metaphysics must begin: at the question of what is eternal, independent, and self-existent.

Historical Challenge 8
Quantum Physics: “Nothing” Is Not Nothing

Some modern appeals to quantum physics claim that events or even universes can arise without causes. The crucial question is what “nothing” means.

The challenge

Quantum cosmology is sometimes used to suggest that the universe can arise from “nothing,” or that quantum events happen without causes.

The reply

Quantum “nothing” is not metaphysical nothing. It is usually a vacuum state, field, law-governed condition, mathematical structure, or physical possibility. That is still some kind of being.

Map conclusion

Quantum physics may challenge older physical pictures of causation, but it does not make being from absolute non-being intelligible. A law-governed “nothing” is already something.

Historical Challenge 9
Existentialism and Postmodernism: Meaning Without Metaphysical Ground

Existentialist and postmodern approaches often shift attention away from metaphysical explanation toward human existence, choice, interpretation, language, or power.

The challenge

Instead of asking what being is and why anything exists, these approaches often ask how meaning is made, how language functions, or how human choice gives shape to life.

The reply

Meaning, choice, and interpretation are activities of beings. They presuppose being; they do not explain why there is being rather than non-being.

Map conclusion

Metaphysics cannot be replaced by meaning-making. The person who interprets, chooses, or constructs is already a being whose existence calls for explanation.

Historical Challenge 10
Buddhism, Lao-Tzu, Wang Pi, and Chuang-Tzu: Non-Being and the Unmanifest

Some non-Western traditions use language of emptiness, non-being, the nameless, or the unmanifest. The key question is whether this means absolute nothing or an indeterminate source.

The challenge

Buddhist and Taoist language can sound as though reality arises from non-being or nothingness. Taoist texts, for example, speak of the nameless or formless source.

The reply

Often “non-being” does not mean absolute nothing. It means the unmanifest, nameless, formless, empty of fixed identity, or beyond ordinary description. In that case, it is not nothing. It is some kind of being.

Map conclusion

If non-being means the unmanifest source, then it is not absolute non-being. If it means absolute nothing, then it cannot produce being. Either way, being from non-being remains impossible.

Positive Case for Theism

Once the false options are eliminated, we are left with the theistic synthesis: some is eternal and some is not eternal. God alone is eternal. Creation is not eternal.

1) Something must be eternal

If nothing were eternal, being would have to come from non-being. That is impossible. Therefore, something must have existed without beginning.

2) What is eternal must be unchanging

Whatever is eternal cannot be moving from potency to act as finite and changing beings do. What is eternal must be fully actual and not dependent on a prior cause.

3) Matter is not eternal

Matter is extended, divisible, and changing. It does not explain itself and cannot be the unchanging ground of all that exists.

4) Finite souls are not eternal

Finite minds learn, forget, deliberate, and change. A finite mind cannot be the infinite, eternal, unchangeable source of reality.

God is a spirit, infinite, eternal, and unchangeable in being, wisdom, power, holiness, justice, goodness, and truth. God alone is the independent Creator of all that is not eternal.

Philosopher reference: J. M. E. McTaggart introduced the influential A-series/B-series distinction. Augustine, Boethius, and Aquinas are central for the theological question of time and eternity.
A-Theory

Past, present, and future are objectively different. The present is real in a special way, and temporal becoming is part of reality.

B-Theory

All moments are equally real, ordered by earlier-than and later-than relations. The present is not metaphysically privileged in the same way.

Why it matters

Theories of time affect how one thinks about change, divine eternity, foreknowledge, providence, and whether God acts in time.

Grounding and Ontological Dependence

Grounding asks what makes something the case. Ontological dependence asks whether a being exists through itself or through another.

Philosopher reference: Classical discussions use participation, causation, and dependence; contemporary metaphysics often uses the language of grounding.
Dependent being

A contingent thing does not explain its own existence. It receives being and depends on causes, conditions, or a deeper ground.

Independent being

What is independent does not receive being from another. In classical theism, God alone is independent, necessary, self-existent being.

If all finite beings are dependent, then the whole system of dependent beings cannot be ultimate. The map therefore moves from contingent beings to God.

Laws of Nature

A law of nature describes or explains the ordered ways created things act. The metaphysical question is what kind of thing a law is.

Philosopher reference: Humean regularity theories treat laws as descriptions of patterns. Aristotelian and powers accounts ground laws in the natures of things. Classical theism grounds order finally in divine wisdom and providence.
Regularity view

Laws summarize repeated patterns: this kind of event is regularly followed by that kind of event.

Governing view

Laws are treated as principles that govern or constrain the behavior of things.

Powers view

Laws express the natures and powers of things. Fire burns because of what fire is; minds think because of what minds are.

Theistic view

The order of nature is not independent of God. Created things have real secondary causes, but the whole order depends on God’s sustaining providence.

Metaphysics Toolkit
Free Will

Free will belongs in metaphysics because it asks what kind of being a human person is. Is the will free only if it is uncaused and able to do otherwise with all things remaining the same, or is the will free when a rational person acts from his own will without restriction?

Philosopher reference: Augustine and Jonathan Edwards are important for compatibilist accounts of will and desire. Thomas Reid, Roderick Chisholm, and Robert Kane help represent libertarian and agent-causal approaches. Harry Frankfurt is central for Frankfurt-style cases.
View 1: PAP — Principle of Alternative Possibilities

This view says you are free only if you could have done otherwise, all things being exactly the same. Freedom is defined by an alternative possibility at the moment of choice.

View 2: Free Will as an Unrestricted Will

This view says the will is free when the person can act according to his will without restriction. Freedom is not the absence of all causes; it is the absence of coercive restraint upon the will.

Core metaphysical issue: Does free choice require uncaused events, or can a choice be both caused and free because it proceeds from the person’s own will, reasons, desires, and character?

The PAP Family

Libertarian Free Will

Libertarianism says we are free because we can do otherwise, all things being the same. In its strongest form, it treats the will as uncaused at the point of choice.

Agent Causation

Agent-causation theories try to preserve libertarian freedom by saying that choices may be influenced by values or beliefs, but the agent as agent is uncaused in making the choice.

Hard Determinism

Hard determinism agrees with the PAP definition: if freedom requires the ability to do otherwise all things being the same, then determinism would mean we are never free.

Compatibilism / Soft Determinism

Compatibilism says that there are no uncaused events. Human choices are real choices because they proceed from the person’s own will. The will is free when the person can act on it without restriction.

1. Practical restriction

I want to leave, but I cannot because the door is locked.

2. Political restriction

I want to leave, but it is illegal.

3. Psychological restriction

I want to leave, but I am afraid to open the door because the last time I did, something bad happened to me.

4. Worldview restriction

I want to leave, but leaving is contrary to my worldview.

5. Presuppositional restriction

I can choose between the two basic presuppositions, but once I do, the rest of my worldview follows with consistency.

6. Reason

I can use reason to determine which presupposition is meaningful — free from contradictions.

Compatibilist reply to libertarianism: The libertarian asks for a kind of freedom only God can have. The error is a confusion of primary and secondary causes. God is the primary cause as Creator. Created things, including gravity and the human will, are real secondary causes.
Free Will
Frankfurt Style Cases

Frankfurt style cases are thought experiments designed to challenge the Principle of Alternative Possibilities. They argue that a person can be morally responsible even when he could not have done otherwise.

Imagine a person chooses to perform an action from his own will. Unknown to him, someone else is prepared to intervene if he begins to choose otherwise. But the intervener never acts because the person chooses the action on his own.

In that case, the person could not have done otherwise in the broad setup of the case, because the intervener would have prevented any alternative. Yet the person still seems responsible because the action came from his own will.

The lesson is that moral responsibility does not require an uncaused will or the ability to do otherwise with all things being the same. Responsibility requires that the act truly belongs to the person as a willing and reasoning agent.

Metaphysical point: Frankfurt cases help separate two questions: whether a person had alternative possibilities and whether the person acted from his own will.

Free Will
Predestination and Free Will

Predestination does not mean human choices are unreal. It means God, as the primary cause, governs all things according to His decree while creatures act as real secondary causes.

Primary cause

God is the Creator, Sustainer, and Governor of all things. His decree is ultimate and independent.

Secondary causes

Created things really cause effects according to their natures. Fire burns, gravity pulls, minds think, and wills choose.

Human responsibility

A person is responsible when the act proceeds from his own will, knowledge, desire, and judgment. Predestination does not force a person to act against what he wants.

The mistake to avoid

Do not treat God’s causality and creaturely causality as rival causes on the same level. God’s primary causality establishes rather than cancels secondary causality.

Free Will
Divine Foreknowledge and Free Will

Divine foreknowledge means God knows future choices. But knowing that a choice will occur is not the same as coercing the will against itself.

Philosopher reference: Augustine, Boethius, Aquinas, Calvin, Turretin, and Edwards all help frame the relation between divine knowledge, divine decree, and real secondary causes.
Knowledge does not coerce

If God knows what I will choose, that does not mean I am dragged into choosing it against my will. The choice can still proceed from me.

Certainty is not compulsion

A future act may be certain in God’s knowledge without being externally compelled in the agent’s experience.

Foreknowledge and desire

God’s knowledge includes the whole person: motives, reasons, desires, beliefs, and circumstances. He knows the act as the act of that person.

Compatibilist conclusion

Foreknowledge threatens libertarian PAP only if freedom means uncaused alternative possibility. It does not threaten the freedom of an unrestricted will acting according to its own desire and judgment.

Necessary and Contingent Being

A necessary being cannot fail to exist. A contingent being exists, but does not have to exist and depends on another for its existence.

Philosopher reference: Avicenna, Aquinas, Leibniz, Samuel Clarke, and later modal philosophers use this distinction to ask why anything contingent exists at all. Leibniz’s principle of sufficient reason belongs near this discussion.
Necessary

God is necessary being: self-existent, independent, and not caused by another.

Contingent

Creatures are contingent: they might not have existed, and their existence must be received from another.

Connection to the main path

If finite things are contingent, they cannot be the eternal source of reality. The argument moves toward God as necessary being.

Modality

Modality studies possible, impossible, necessary, and contingent being. It asks not only what is, but what could be, what must be, and what cannot be.

Possible

A state of affairs is possible if it does not involve contradiction.

Impossible

A square-circle is impossible because it contradicts itself.

Necessary

What is necessary cannot fail to be true or to exist.

Contingent

What is contingent is real or possible, but not self-explanatory.

Modality
Logically Possible Worlds

When we inquire about logically possible worlds, we start with the most basic features of a world and not less basic features.

For instance, a professor said to his Ph.D. student, “There is a logically possible world of only ping-pong balls.” But that world is a world in which only matter exists. Therefore, if material monism cannot be true, then there is no world of only ping-pong balls.

To know what is a logically possible world, we must know what is eternal.

Another example of less basic issues are questions like, “Could there be one more star or one more grain of sand in the world God created?” To answer this, you must know teleology: What is the chief end of the creation?

Contemporary Ontology
Multiverse

Multiverse theory says that what we call “the universe” is not the whole of material reality. Our observable cosmos may be only one region, bubble, branch, or world among many.

A multiverse theory may multiply worlds, but it does not by itself answer the question of this map: what is eternal, permanent, and unchanging? Many universes are still beings. If they exist, they still require an account of their being, order, unity, and dependence.

1. What Multiverse Theory Claims

Physical multiverse

In physics, a multiverse proposal usually means that our observable cosmos is one part of a larger physical order. This might be described as many cosmic regions, many inflationary “bubble” universes, or many branches produced by interpretations of quantum mechanics.

Modal multiverse

In philosophy, “many worlds” language can also refer to possible worlds: ways reality could have been. Some philosophers treat possible worlds as abstract descriptions. Others, most famously David Lewis, treat them as concrete realities.

Metaphysical warning

Neither kind of multiverse should be confused with an explanation of being itself. A larger physical system still needs to be explained. A set of possible worlds still depends on what is truly possible, impossible, necessary, and contingent.

2. Major Figures

David Lewis

Lewis defended modal realism: possible worlds are real, concrete worlds, though causally isolated from ours. His view is important for the metaphysical version of “many worlds,” even though it is not the same as a scientific cosmological multiverse.

Robert Nozick

Nozick explored a “principle of fecundity,” the idea that reality might include many or all possible worlds. This is another philosophical attempt to account for why there is something rather than nothing by appealing to maximal plenitude.

John Leslie

Leslie developed philosophical arguments about many worlds, value, and creation. His work is useful because it shows how multiverse speculation often becomes a metaphysical and theological question, not merely a scientific one.

Hugh Everett, David Deutsch, Andrei Linde, Leonard Susskind, and Max Tegmark

These are major scientific or scientific-philosophical figures associated with many-worlds quantum theory, inflationary cosmology, string landscape theory, and mathematical universe proposals. They show that “multiverse” can mean several different things.

3. The Ambiguity of “Universe”

The word universe contains the idea of the uni of the diverse: the unity of diversity. A universe is the ordered whole, the sum total of connected material parts.

This creates an ambiguity in the word multiverse. Does it mean many connected regions within one larger material whole, or does it mean wholly separate realities that never meet and have no connection at all?

If the multiverse is a tangle of connected universes, then all those connected parts form one larger order. In that case, the “multiverse” is really one big universe: the unity of all connected material diversity.

But if the multiverse is a series of parallel universes that never meet and are not connected in any way, then we cannot know them by observation, causal contact, inference from effects, or any relation to us. If they are not connected even by our knowing about them, then we cannot responsibly say that they exist.

Therefore, the multiverse proposal often trades on the ambiguity of “universe.” Connected universes are one universe. Absolutely disconnected universes are not knowable as existing universes.

4. Does a Multiverse Explain What Is Eternal?

If the multiverse is material

Then it belongs under the old question of material monism: is matter eternal? Calling matter “many universes” does not explain why matter exists, why it is ordered, or why its laws hold.

If the multiverse changes

Then it is not eternal in the full metaphysical sense of being permanent and unchanging. It may be very large, ancient, or cyclic, but that is not the same thing as being necessary, self-existent, and immutable.

If the multiverse is governed by laws

Then the laws are not explained merely by multiplying universes. The map must still ask whether law, order, number, possibility, and intelligibility depend on something beyond the material order.

5. Identity Across Universes

Multiverse and possible-worlds language also raises the problem of identity: what would make the “same person” exist in another universe?

The Socrates problem

Could Socrates have had the body of an alligator in another universe? If the answer is yes, then “Socrates” seems to lose any stable essence. If the answer is no, then there are limits on what counts as the same individual across worlds.

Saul Kripke

Kripke argued that proper names are rigid designators: “Socrates” names that very man, not just whoever satisfies a description. This supports the idea that some features, especially origin and kind, are essential. Socrates could have lacked some accidental traits, but he could not simply be an alligator and still be Socrates.

Alvin Plantinga

Plantinga defended transworld identity: the same individual can be considered as existing in different possible worlds. On this view, we do not replace Socrates with a similar counterpart; we speak of what Socrates himself could or could not have been.

David Lewis

Lewis rejected strict identity across worlds and proposed counterpart theory. On his account, another world does not contain Socrates himself, but a counterpart sufficiently similar to Socrates. This avoids one problem but raises another: are we still talking about Socrates, or only about someone like him?

Robert Adams and haecceity

Adams helped clarify the idea of thisness, or haecceity: the particular “this individual” character of a person. If persons have thisness, then identity is not reducible to a bundle of qualities that could be rearranged endlessly across worlds.

Conclusion: The multiverse does not remove the need for metaphysics. It either describes one larger connected universe, or it describes disconnected worlds we cannot know. And once it begins speaking of possible versions of persons, it must face the old metaphysical questions of essence, identity, possibility, and what makes a thing the same thing.

Dimensions

Talk about other dimensions can be useful in mathematics and physics, but dimensions are not automatically ultimate realities. A dimension is a measurable or describable aspect of a world, not an explanation of why any world exists.

Mathematical description

Dimensions can describe spatial, temporal, or mathematical structure. But a description is not the same as an independent being.

Metaphysical question

If dimensions exist, are they created features of reality, abstract descriptions, or parts of a physical system? They still require a prior account of being.

Connection to the main question

Adding dimensions does not solve the problem of what is eternal. It only adds more structure that must be explained.

Metaphysics 101
Properties and Accidents

A substance exists in itself. A property or accident exists in another. Redness, height, location, and motion do not float by themselves; they belong to some being.

Philosopher reference: Aristotle’s Categories is the classic starting point for substance and accidents.
Substance

The thing that exists: a tree, a person, a horse, a stone.

Accident / property

What belongs to a substance: color, size, relation, location, action, or passion.

Why this matters

It keeps us from treating qualities, relations, or mathematical descriptions as if they were self-existent beings.

Metaphysics 101
Mind and Body

The mind/body problem asks how conscious thought relates to the body. Is the human person merely material, merely spiritual, or an embodied rational soul?

Philosopher reference: Plato and Descartes are associated with strong dualism; Aristotle and Aquinas with the soul as the form of the body; modern materialists reduce mind to matter. Idealists and spiritual monists reduce matter to mind. The Aristotleian view of substance/essence as what makes a thing what it is leaves us with the need to still find the substance as the being that underlies the properties we experience. Is this being extended and non-conscious (matter) or non-extended and conscious (spirit)?
Materialism

The mind is reduced to the brain or physical processes.

Dualism

Mind and body are distinct, though they must be related in the human person.

Christian anthropology

Human persons are embodied rational souls. The body is not an illusion or prison; it belongs to the created good.

Memory

Metaphysics 101
Aseity, Simplicity, and Immutability

These doctrines explain why God is not merely the biggest being, but the independent and eternal source of all created being. They are related to the incommunicable attributes of God (infinite, eternal, unchangeable). Whatever has existed from eternity cannot change (grow or deline) and is that than which there is no greater (infinite). Within those truths lies that "otherness" of God which explains why he alone is worthy of worship and why idolatry of any kind is a logical error and a sin. Also within those attributes is the proof of the clarity of God's existence. It is clear that God exists becuase something must be eternal but whatever is changing cannot be eternal. The material world and finite souls are changing and therefore cannot be eternal (without beginning, although they could be without end).

Philosopher reference: Augustine, Anselm, Aquinas, Calvin, and Reformed scholastic theologians such as Turretin are important here.

Theistic arguments: The ontological argument says that God is the greatest conceivable being and that such a being cannot exist merely in the mind but must exist in reality. The cosmological argument says that contingent or caused beings require a necessary or first cause. The teleological argument says that order, design, and purposiveness in the world point to an intelligent designer. These have been challenged for overextending from premise to conclusion: if they prove anything, it is less than “God.” Perhaps if they are taken as steps building to a goal rather than as complete in themselves, they can show the clarity of God’s existence.

Aseity

God exists from himself and depends on nothing outside himself.

Simplicity

God is not composed of parts. He is not assembled from more basic realities. This does not mean God is simplistic or empty; it means God is not made out of matter, form, properties, act, potency, or any dependent constituents.

Immutability

God does not change, develop, improve, decay, or move from potency to act.

Connection

Use these to prove that God alone has existed from eternity by showing that "eternal dependence" is a contradiction

Metaphysics 101
Creation Ex Nihilo

Creation from nothing does not mean that nothing produces being. It means God creates without using preexistent material.

False meaning

Being came from non-being by itself. This is impossible, because nothing has no power to produce anything.

True meaning

God, who is eternal being, freely gives existence to creatures without needing eternal matter.

Why this matters

It distinguishes Christian creation from both materialism and pagan eternal-matter views.
