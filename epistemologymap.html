Definition

Epistemology is the study of knowledge. It asks what it means to know something, whether knowledge is possible, and how we can distinguish truth from error.

Main Path — Stage 4
The Three Acts of the Mind

How Reason works

Reason acts by forming concepts, joining concepts into propositions, and drawing inferences from propositions. This is the methodological core of the map.

There are three acts of the mind. We use our minds to form concepts, propositions, and inferences.

Act of the Mind
Concepts

We use the laws of thought to form concepts. We identify A is A, we distinguish A from non-A, and we sort everything into either A or non-A.

In a concept, the mind uses Reason to grasp the essence of a being, class of beings, activity, or property. The essence of a thing is the set of qualities that all members and only members always have.

A concept is not the same as a word. A concept is universal whereas words are particular to a language. A word is a conventional sign attached to a concept. We can learn foreign languages because they are using signs to express the same universal concepts we are using signs to express. We can learn each other's signs and communicate concepts to each other.

Application 1: Complex behavior is not the same as thought. Behaviorists, like Skinner, reduce thinking to behavior and then use pleasure or pain to modify behavior without addressing the mind.

Application 2: If animals could think then we could communicate with them as we do with other rational thinkers and their language. It would simply be a matter of learning the signs they are attaching to concepts. Yet, while we can pick up a few words in a foreign language within minutes, we have lived with animals for centuries and cannot have rational communication with them. Their sounds communicate but their sounds are not rational language.

Application 3: A.I. Is artificial thinking real thinking? The answer is in the name. Artificial intelligence is the appearance of intelligence on a computer screen.

Next, we use Reason to put concepts together into propositions.

Application: Moral Concepts

If concepts grasp essences, then we can ask whether the mind can form true concepts of good, evil, human nature, duty, and law. This leads to the question of natural law and moral knowledge.

Act of the Mind
Propositions

A proposition is the act of the mind in which concepts are joined together in a statement that can be true or false. The basic structure of a proposition is subject, copula, and predicate.

The subject is what the proposition is about. The copula connects or denies a connection. The predicate is what is being affirmed or denied of the subject. In the simplest form, a proposition says S is P or S is not P.

Aristotle explains how propositions work in Prior Analytics and On Interpretation. In On Interpretation, Aristotle studies names, verbs, affirmation, denial, and the kinds of statements that can be true or false. In Prior Analytics, he studies how propositions are arranged into syllogisms so that one proposition follows from others.

1. Subject, Copula, Predicate

Every categorical proposition has three logical parts. In “All men are mortal,” the subject is men, the copula is are, and the predicate is mortal. In “No triangles are circles,” the subject is triangles, the copula is are not, and the predicate is circles.

The copula is not merely grammar. It performs the logical act of either joining the subject to the predicate or denying that the subject belongs to the predicate. This is why propositions are truth-bearers: they affirm or deny something about what is.

2. Quantity: Universal, Particular, Singular

The quantity of a proposition tells us how much of the subject is being spoken of. A universal proposition speaks of the whole subject class. A particular proposition speaks of some member or members of the class. A singular proposition speaks of one named subject.

Universal words include all, every, each, and no. Particular words include some, at least one, many, or a when it means “one of.” Names such as Socrates are singular subjects.

Sometimes the quantity is implied rather than written. “Men are mortal” normally means “All men are mortal.” “Man is rational” normally means “All men are rational,” because it speaks about human nature. But “A man is at the door” is particular, because it means one man, not all mankind.

Abstract objects can also be subjects. “Squareness is a shape-property” speaks about squareness itself. “Justice is good” speaks about justice as an intelligible object of thought. The mind can form true or false propositions about beings, classes, activities, properties, and abstract ideas.

3. Quality: Affirmative or Negative

The quality of a proposition tells us whether the predicate is affirmed or denied of the subject. An affirmative proposition says that S is P. A negative proposition says that S is not P.

“All men are mortal” is affirmative. “No men are angels” is negative. “Some men are philosophers” is affirmative. “Some men are not philosophers” is negative.

4. The Four Categorical Forms

Classical categorical logic classifies propositions by quantity and quality. This gives four standard forms:

A — Universal Affirmative: All S is P.
E — Universal Negative: No S is P.
I — Particular Affirmative: Some S is P.
O — Particular Negative: Some S is not P.

Singular propositions such as “Socrates is a philosopher” are not A, E, I, or O in the strict categorical form, but they are often treated like universal claims for purposes of inference because the whole named subject is being spoken of.

5. Distribution: When a Term Is Taken Universally

A term is distributed when the proposition speaks about the whole extension of that term. The key rules are:

A: All S is P — distributes the subject, not the predicate.
E: No S is P — distributes both subject and predicate.
I: Some S is P — distributes neither subject nor predicate.
O: Some S is not P — distributes the predicate, not the subject.

This is why “All men are mortal” does not mean “All mortal beings are men.” The predicate is not distributed. But “No men are angels” excludes the whole class of men from the whole class of angels, so both terms are distributed.

How to Analyze Any Proposition

To analyze a proposition, ask these questions in order:

1. What is the subject?
2. What is the copula?
3. What is the predicate?
4. Is the quantity universal, particular, or singular?
5. Is the quality affirmative or negative?
6. If it is categorical, is it A, E, I, or O?

Once the proposition has been analyzed, it can be compared with other propositions and used in arguments. This leads to the third act of the mind: inference.

Main Path — Stage 5
Inferences

From propositions to necessity

Inference shows that some propositions follow necessarily from other propositions. Logic studies these necessary relations and prepares the transition from epistemology to what is eternal.

Inferences are also called arguments. In an argument, you have propositions that serve as premises, and from those premises you infer another proposition called the conclusion. The classic form uses two premises and a conclusion. This is called a syllogism.

Syllogisms can be studied in Aristotle's Prior Analytics. Aristotle shows how propositions can be arranged so that one proposition follows from others.

The Structure of a Syllogism

A categorical syllogism has three terms. The major term is the predicate of the conclusion. The minor term is the subject of the conclusion. The middle term appears in both premises but not in the conclusion. The middle term is what joins, or separates, the subject and predicate in the conclusion.

For example:
All men are mortal.
Socrates is a man.
Therefore, Socrates is mortal.

In this argument, “mortal” is the major term, “Socrates” is the minor term, and “man” is the middle term. The middle term connects Socrates to mortality because Socrates belongs to the class man, and all men belong to the class mortal.

Mood, Figure, and Form

Each categorical proposition has one of four basic forms: A = All S is P, E = No S is P, I = Some S is P, and O = Some S is not P. The mood of a syllogism is the sequence of these three proposition-types: major premise, minor premise, and conclusion. For example, Barbara has the mood AAA: All M is P; All S is M; therefore All S is P.

The figure of a syllogism tells us where the middle term appears in the two premises. In the first figure, the middle term is subject of the major premise and predicate of the minor premise: M–P, S–M, therefore S–P. The other figures rearrange the middle term: second figure P–M / S–M; third figure M–P / M–S; fourth figure P–M / M–S.

The form of a syllogism is its mood together with its figure. A syllogism can have true-sounding words and still be invalid if its form is invalid. Logic lets us test the form instead of being carried along by the rhetoric.

Validity and Soundness

An argument is valid when, if the premises are true, the conclusion must be true. Validity is about form. It does not tell us whether the premises are actually true. It tells us whether the conclusion follows from them.

An argument is sound when it is valid and its premises are true. If an argument is sound, then the conclusion is true. So validity preserves truth from the premises to the conclusion, and soundness gives us truth because the premises really are true.

How Many Syllogisms Are There?

In classical categorical logic there are 256 possible syllogistic forms: four possible proposition-types for each of the three lines of the argument, across four figures. That is 4 × 4 × 4 × 4 = 256.

Most of these are invalid. Traditionally, logicians identify 24 valid forms when the older Aristotelian assumption of existential import is allowed. In many modern presentations, only 15 are unconditionally valid, because universal propositions are not assumed to imply that their subjects actually exist. Another common medieval list gives 19 principal valid moods, with several additional weakened forms.

The valid traditional forms are commonly named this way:

First Figure: Barbara, Celarent, Darii, Ferio; plus Barbari and Celaront as weakened forms.
Second Figure: Cesare, Camestres, Festino, Baroco; plus Cesaro and Camestrop as weakened forms.
Third Figure: Darapti, Disamis, Datisi, Felapton, Bocardo, Ferison.
Fourth Figure: Bramantip, Camenes, Dimaris, Fesapo, Fresison; plus Camenop as a weakened form.

The remaining possible forms are invalid because they break rules such as these: the middle term must be distributed at least once; a term distributed in the conclusion must be distributed in the premise; two negative premises prove nothing; a negative conclusion requires one negative premise; and two particular premises prove nothing in the traditional categorical system.

The Square of Opposition

The square of opposition diagrams the four categorical propositions: A = All S is P, E = No S is P, I = Some S is P, and O = Some S is not P. It shows how these propositions relate to each other.

AAll S is P
ENo S is P
ISome S is P
OSome S is not P

A and E are contraries. I and O are subcontraries. A contradicts O, and E contradicts I. Subalternation runs from A to I and from E to O, assuming the subject exists.

A and O are contradictories: they cannot both be true and cannot both be false. E and I are also contradictories. A and E are contraries: they cannot both be true, though they can both be false. I and O are subcontraries: in the traditional square, they cannot both be false, though they can both be true. Subalternation runs from universal to particular: if A is true, I follows; if E is true, O follows, assuming the subject exists.

The square helps us see what follows when one proposition is true or false. Then syllogisms let us arrange propositions into arguments to determine what else must be true or false. In this way, the mind moves from concepts, to propositions, to inferences, and then to knowledge by proof.

Question 4
How do you know a proposition is true?

Once we agree that knowledge is propositional, we must ask how the mind knows that a proposition is true. Many answers identify one source of information and treat it as sufficient. But each of these answers depends on deeper acts of reason: forming concepts, judging propositions, and drawing inferences.

The mistaken answers below are important side paths. They each contain something useful, but none of them can finally explain how a proposition is known to be true.

The Right Path

Learn how to use your mind to know to build propositions and determine if they are true or false.

Mistaken Answer
My Senses (Empiricism)

Definition

Empiricism says that knowledge comes from sense experience. On this answer, a proposition is known to be true because it is seen, heard, touched, measured, or otherwise experienced through the senses.

Why It Fails as the Final Answer

The senses give material for knowledge, but they do not by themselves judge propositions. Sense experience must be interpreted. The eye may see color and shape, but the mind forms the concept, joins subject and predicate, and judges whether the proposition is true.

Illusions also show the problem. Railroad tracks appear to meet at the horizon, and an oar appears bent in water. The senses must be corrected by reasoned judgment.

Push Forward

Sense experience is useful, but it cannot explain the act by which the mind knows a proposition to be true. That requires the acts of the mind.

Mistaken Answer
Common Sense

Definition

Common sense appeals to what ordinary people take to be obvious: the external world, other minds, memory, moral responsibility, and the basic reliability of everyday life.

Why It Fails as the Final Answer

Common sense may identify truths that skepticism must explain away, but merely saying “everyone knows this” does not explain how a proposition is known to be true. Common sense can also be confused, culturally shaped, or corrected by deeper reflection.

Push Forward

Common sense needs an account of the mind’s operation. The question is not merely what seems obvious, but how the mind forms, judges, and reasons about what is true.

Mistaken Answer
Intuition

Definition

Intuition appeals to immediate seeming: “I just see that this is true.” It treats direct apprehension or strong intellectual appearance as the source of knowledge.

Why It Fails as the Final Answer

Intuitions conflict. What seems obvious to one person may seem false to another. A strong seeming is not yet a demonstrated judgment. Intuition must be tested by concepts, propositions, and inferences.

Push Forward

Immediate insight may begin inquiry, but it does not replace the need to understand how the mind judges truth.

Mistaken Answer
Tradition

Definition

Tradition appeals to inherited teaching, authorities, institutions, customs, and texts. It says that a proposition is known because it has been handed down.

Why It Fails as the Final Answer

Tradition can preserve truth, but it can also preserve error. To know whether a tradition is true, the mind must judge the proposition it transmits. Otherwise, rival traditions would all be equally authoritative.

Push Forward

Tradition may be a means of receiving propositions, but reason must still understand, judge, and infer whether they are true.

Mistaken Answer
Empirical Science

Definition

Empirical science studies the natural world by observation, measurement, experiment, hypothesis, and correction.

Why It Fails as the Final Answer

Science is powerful, but it does not explain all knowledge. Scientific inquiry already presupposes logic, mathematics, causality, the reliability of reason, the intelligibility of nature, and the distinction between truth and error. None of these is established by a laboratory experiment alone.

Push Forward

Science uses propositions and inferences. Therefore, the deeper question is how the mind forms true propositions and draws valid inferences.

Mistaken Answer
Reason (Rationalism)

Definition

Rationalism emphasizes reason, necessary truths, innate ideas, and deduction. It rightly sees that the mind is not a passive receiver of sensations.

Why It Fails as the Final Answer

Reason is necessary, but rationalism can become a mistaken answer when it treats reason as an autonomous source detached from reality, creation, and God. If reason is made self-grounding, then the map has not explained why reason reaches truth rather than merely arranging ideas inside the mind.

Push Forward

The right move is not to reject reason, but to understand how reason acts: it forms concepts, judges propositions, and draws inferences.

Mistaken Answer
Fideism

Definition

Fideism treats faith as independent of, above, or even opposed to reason. It answers the question of truth by saying, in effect, “I believe by faith, not by reason.”

Why It Fails as the Final Answer

Fideism cannot escape propositions. To say “faith is above reason” is itself a proposition. The person who says this must still know what the words mean, judge the statement to be true, and distinguish it from its denial.

Faith also has an object. If faith is not directed toward what is true, then it becomes mere will, feeling, or preference.

Push Forward

Faith does not abolish the mind. The mind must form concepts, understand propositions, and draw inferences about what is believed.

Skepticism Node
Informal Fallacies

Definition

Informal fallacies are mistakes in reasoning where an argument fails because of relevance, ambiguity, emotional manipulation, false alternatives, or defective appeals rather than because of its formal structure.

Failure Mode

A person discovers that many arguments are fallacious and then concludes that all reasoning is manipulation. The failure is moving from “some arguments are bad” to “argument itself cannot give knowledge.”

Main Philosophers and Claims

Aristotle (384–322 BC): Taught at the Lyceum in Athens. In Sophistical Refutations, he analyzed deceptive arguments and helped establish the study of fallacies.
John Stuart Mill (1806–1873): Lived and worked in London and served in Parliament; he was not a university professor. In A System of Logic, he discussed fallacies, induction, and methods of reasoning.

Push back toward Knowledge: Bad reasoning does not refute reasoning. It shows that the mind must learn to distinguish valid arguments from invalid ones.

The problem is that intuitions conflict. What seems obvious to one person may seem false to another. If intuition is treated as the final source of knowledge, disagreement can produce skepticism.

Main Philosophers and Claims

René Descartes (1596–1650): Lived chiefly in France, the Dutch Republic, and Sweden; he was not a university professor. He appealed to clear and distinct perception as a mark of certainty.
Henri Bergson (1859–1941): Taught at the Collège de France in Paris. He gave intuition a major role in grasping reality beyond static conceptual analysis.

Push back toward Knowledge: Immediate awareness may be important, but we still need to know why the intuition is true and not merely strongly felt.

Skepticism Node
Tradition

Definition

Tradition is knowledge received through inherited teaching, customs, institutions, texts, and communities.

Failure Mode

Tradition can preserve wisdom, but it can also preserve error. A person may discover a false tradition and then conclude that all inherited knowledge is unreliable.

Main Philosophers and Claims

Edmund Burke (1729–1797): Lived and worked mainly in London as a statesman and writer; he was not a university professor. He defended inherited social wisdom against abstract revolutionary rationalism.
Alasdair MacIntyre (1929–): Taught at several universities, including the University of Notre Dame. He argued that rational inquiry occurs within traditions, not from a neutral nowhere.

Push back toward Knowledge: A false tradition does not prove that tradition cannot transmit knowledge. It proves that traditions must be tested by truth.

Skepticism Node
Science

Definition

Science is disciplined inquiry into the natural world through observation, measurement, hypothesis, experiment, and correction.

Failure Mode

Science becomes a source of skepticism when people expect it to provide absolute certainty and then discover that scientific conclusions are revisable. The problem is worse when science is treated as the only source of knowledge, since science itself depends on non-scientific assumptions.

Main Philosophers and Claims

Francis Bacon (1561–1626): Lived and worked in England as a lawyer, statesman, and writer; he was not a university professor. He promoted experimental inquiry and the disciplined study of nature.
Karl Popper (1902–1994): Taught at the London School of Economics. He argued that scientific theories should be falsifiable rather than finally verified.
Thomas Kuhn (1922–1996): Taught at the University of California, Berkeley, Princeton University, and MIT. He argued that scientific communities work within paradigms that can undergo revolutionary shifts.

Push back toward Knowledge: Science gives real knowledge only if there is an ordered world, reliable reason, reliable perception, and stable laws. Those assumptions need philosophical explanation.

Skepticism Node
Reason

Definition

Reason is the power of the mind to form concepts, judge propositions, and draw conclusions.

Failure Mode

Reason becomes a source of skepticism when people notice contradictions, invalid arguments, and endless philosophical disagreement. They then conclude that reason cannot reach truth.

Main Philosophers and Claims

Plato (428/427–348/347 BC): Lived in Athens and founded the Academy. He argued that knowledge requires grasping stable intelligible realities beyond mere opinion.
Aristotle (384–322 BC): Taught at the Lyceum in Athens. He developed logic as an instrument for distinguishing valid from invalid reasoning.
Immanuel Kant (1724–1804): Taught at the University of Königsberg. He argued that reason has limits and that the mind structures experience through categories.

Push back toward Knowledge: To argue against reason, one must use reason. So reason cannot be globally rejected without self-defeat.

Skepticism Node
Academic Skepticism

Definition

Academic Skepticism developed in Plato's Academy and held that certain knowledge is unavailable to human beings, especially in disputed philosophical matters.

Failure Mode

Its failure mode is that it replaces knowledge with probability or plausibility. But if we only have probability, then we must ask how we know what is probable and what standard judges it.

Main Philosophers and Claims

Arcesilaus (316/315–241/240 BC): Lived and taught in Athens as head of Plato's Academy. He attacked Stoic claims to certain cognition.
Carneades (214/213–129/128 BC): Lived and taught in Athens as head of the Academy. He developed a probabilistic approach to belief and criticized dogmatic certainty.
Cicero (106–43 BC): Lived in Rome and worked as a statesman, lawyer, and writer; he was not a professor. He transmitted Academic Skepticism to the Roman world.

Push back toward Knowledge: If skepticism says we should follow what is probable, it must explain how probability is known.

Skepticism Node
Pyrrhonism

Definition

Pyrrhonism teaches that disputed questions often have apparently equal arguments on both sides. The Pyrrhonist suspends judgment and seeks tranquility by refusing dogmatic commitment.

Failure Mode

Its failure mode is paralysis. If one suspends judgment about everything, one cannot consistently claim that suspension itself is wise, true, or better.

Main Philosophers and Claims

Pyrrho of Elis (c. 360–270 BC): Lived in Elis in Greece; he was not a university professor. He became the namesake of Pyrrhonian skepticism.
Sextus Empiricus (c. 160–210 AD): Lived in the Greco-Roman world, probably Alexandria, Rome, or Athens; he was a physician rather than a professor. His writings preserve the most extensive account of Pyrrhonism.

Push back toward Knowledge: Suspending judgment may expose bad claims, but it cannot become a complete way of life without smuggling in knowledge claims.

Skepticism Node
Cartesian Skepticism

Definition

Cartesian Skepticism uses radical doubt as a method. It asks whether the senses deceive, whether waking life could be like dreaming, and whether even mathematics could be manipulated by a deceiver.

Failure Mode

Its failure mode is that the desire for absolute certainty can make ordinary knowledge appear impossible. If every possible doubt defeats knowledge, then almost nothing can be known.

Main Philosophers and Claims

René Descartes (1596–1650): Lived chiefly in France, the Dutch Republic, and Sweden; he was not a university professor. He argued that “I think, therefore I am” survives radical doubt.
Princess Elisabeth of Bohemia (1618–1680): Lived in the Dutch Republic and later in Herford Abbey; she was not a professor. Her correspondence with Descartes pressed him on the difficulty of mind-body interaction.

Push back toward Knowledge: The fact that something can be doubted does not by itself prove that it is not known.

Humean Skepticism

Definition

Humean Skepticism questions causation, induction, the self, miracles, and religious belief. It argues that many beliefs we treat as rationally proven are actually habits of expectation.

David Hume (1711–1776): Lived chiefly in Edinburgh and worked as a historian, librarian, and diplomat; he was not a university professor. He argued that induction cannot be justified by reason without circularity.
Thomas Reid (1710–1796): Taught at King's College Aberdeen and the University of Glasgow. He developed Common Sense philosophy partly in response to Hume.

Skepticism Node
Kantian Skepticism

Definition

Kantian Skepticism is not the denial of all knowledge. It says that we know things as they appear to us under the forms and categories of the mind, not things as they are in themselves.

Failure Mode

Its failure mode is that it creates a gap between appearance and reality. If we cannot know things as they are in themselves, then metaphysical knowledge of God, the soul, and the world becomes deeply limited.

Main Philosophers and Claims

Immanuel Kant (1724–1804): Taught at the University of Königsberg. His critical philosophy sought to answer Hume while limiting speculative metaphysical knowledge.
Friedrich Heinrich Jacobi (1743–1819): Lived and worked in Germany as a writer and public intellectual; he was not a professor. He criticized rationalist systems and helped frame post-Kantian debates about faith and reason.

Push back toward Knowledge: If the mind structures experience, we must still ask how we know that claim and whether it undercuts itself.

Skepticism Node
Nihilism

Definition

Nihilism is the collapse or denial of meaning, truth, morality, or value. It often follows when skepticism spreads from one source of knowledge to all knowledge and all value.

Failure Mode

Its failure mode is self-destruction. If there is no truth, then nihilism is not true. If there is no good, then there is no reason to prefer nihilism over knowledge.

Main Philosophers and Claims

Friedrich Nietzsche (1844–1900): Taught classical philology at the University of Basel before leaving academic life. He diagnosed European nihilism as the cultural result of the “death of God.”
Martin Heidegger (1889–1976): Taught at the University of Marburg and the University of Freiburg. He interpreted nihilism as connected to the history of Western metaphysics and forgetfulness of Being.

Push back toward Knowledge: Nihilism shows where total skepticism leads, but it cannot justify itself as true or better.

Main path

We have moved from the collapse of false confidence to the rebuilding of knowledge. Knowledge requires belief, truth, and adequate evidence. The next step is Reason, because Reason is what forms concepts, judges propositions, and draws inferences.

Knowledge is a belief, meaning that the mind affirms a proposition such as “S is P.” It is not enough simply to have a belief. The belief must be true, meaning that the person believes “S is P” and “S is P” is indeed the case. And the belief must have adequate evidence: the proof or demonstration that it is indeed true.

You can read about this definition and its explanation in Charles Hodge.

We use the word knowledge in a few different ways. Knowing about a person is not the same as knowing the person. Knowing about how to ride a bike is not the same as knowing how to ride a bike. These are often called relational knowledge, skill knowledge, and propositional knowledge.

When we are asked a question about either of the first two, we show that we know by giving a propositional answer. Consider a man who is asked to pick his friend out of a lineup. He could point to the friend in the group and say, “That's him.” But if follow-up questions were asked, he would reply with propositions he believes are true about his friend.

The same is true when being asked about riding a bike. A person might be able to ride a bike but not be able to demonstrate much propositional knowledge about it. We would say that they know how, but that they do not know that.

Contemporary Epistemology
What is Justification?

Justification answers the question: why is your belief reasonable? Knowledge is not merely true belief. It must be supported in the right way. Justification is what connects belief to truth.

A belief is justified when a person has adequate grounds for holding it. These grounds may include evidence, logical reasoning, reliable processes, or properly functioning cognitive faculties.

There are different theories about how justification works:

  • Foundationalism: Some beliefs are basic and support others.
  • Coherentism: Beliefs are justified by fitting into a consistent system.
  • Reliabilism: Beliefs are justified if produced by reliable processes.
  • Infinitism: Justification involves an infinite chain of reasons.
René Descartes (1596–1650): Lived chiefly in France, the Dutch Republic, and Sweden; he was not a university professor. He sought indubitable foundations for knowledge.
W. V. O. Quine (1908–2000): Taught at Harvard University. He emphasized the web-like structure of belief and the way beliefs are tested within a whole system.
Alvin Goldman (1938–): Taught at the University of Arizona and Rutgers University. He developed reliabilism as a major modern theory of justification.
Peter Klein (1940–): Taught at Rutgers University. He defended infinitism as an alternative account of the structure of justification.

The deeper question is this: what ultimately justifies our beliefs? If every belief needs another belief to support it, justification seems to collapse into regress. This pushes us toward something more fundamental.

Contemporary Epistemology
Reliabilism

Reliabilism is the view that a belief is justified, or counts as knowledge, when it is produced by a reliable belief-forming process. The focus is not first on whether the person can explain the proof, but whether the process that produced the belief usually leads to truth.

Reliable processes might include ordinary perception in normal conditions, memory when functioning properly, sound reasoning, or competent testimony. Unreliable processes would include wishful thinking, guessing, hallucination, prejudice, or manipulation.

This makes reliabilism a form of externalism. A person may know something even if he cannot give a full internal account of why he knows it, so long as his belief is produced by a truth-conducive process.

Alvin Goldman (1938–): Taught at the University of Arizona and Rutgers University. Goldman developed process reliabilism and argued that justification depends on whether a belief is caused by a reliable cognitive process.
David Armstrong (1926–2014): Taught at the University of Sydney. Armstrong defended an externalist account of knowledge in which knowledge involves a reliable connection between belief and fact.

Reliabilism helps explain ordinary knowledge. A child may know that the stove is hot by perception, even if he cannot state a formal argument about heat, sensation, and causal reliability.

But reliabilism raises an important question: how do we know that the process is reliable? If we answer by using the same process, we risk circularity. If we answer by using another process, we must ask whether that process is reliable too.

This pushes the student back toward the deeper question of Reason: what makes our minds able to reach truth at all?

Contemporary Epistemology
Epistemic Luck

Epistemic luck occurs when a belief is true, but only by accident. This shows that true belief is not the same as knowledge.

Consider this example: a man believes, “There is a sheep in the field,” because he sees what looks like a sheep. But what he sees is actually a dog disguised as a sheep. Unknown to him, there really is a sheep hidden behind a hill.

His belief is true. It is also justified in one sense, because it is based on perception. But it is still not knowledge. Why? Because it is true by luck, not because his reasoning connects properly to reality.

Edmund Gettier (1927–2021): Worked at Wayne State University and the University of Massachusetts Amherst. His famous 1963 article showed that justified true belief is not sufficient for knowledge.

This led philosophers to propose additional conditions for knowledge:

  • Safety: the belief could not easily have been false.
  • Sensitivity: if it were false, the person would not believe it.

Epistemic luck reveals something deeper: knowledge requires not just truth, but a proper connection between belief and reality.

This pushes us toward the idea that knowledge depends on something stable, not something accidental.

Contemporary Epistemology
Defeaters

A defeater is information that removes, weakens, or overturns the justification for a belief. Even if a person has good reason to believe something, new evidence may defeat that belief.

There are two main kinds of defeaters:

  • Rebutting defeater: evidence that the belief itself is false. For example, you believe the road is open, but then you see a sign saying, “Road Closed.”
  • Undercutting defeater: evidence that your reason for believing is unreliable. For example, you believe the road is open because a friend told you, but then you learn your friend was reading last year’s map.

Defeaters are important because knowledge is not merely having some evidence. It requires having evidence that survives relevant objections. A belief may be reasonable at one moment and no longer reasonable after new information appears.

Roderick Chisholm (1916–1999): Taught at Brown University. Chisholm helped shape contemporary discussions of justification, evidence, and epistemic responsibility.
John Pollock (1940–2009): Taught at the University of Arizona. Pollock developed influential accounts of defeasible reasoning and distinguished between kinds of defeaters.

Defeaters also help explain why intellectual humility matters. A person should not cling to a belief after the evidence for it has been defeated. But not every objection is a real defeater. Some objections are merely doubts, distractions, or skeptical possibilities.

The key question is: what counts as a legitimate defeater? To answer that, we need standards of reason, evidence, and truth. So the study of defeaters points us back to the laws of thought and the proper use of Reason.

Contemporary Epistemology
Responses to Skepticism

Skepticism argues that knowledge is impossible, uncertain, or far less common than we ordinarily think. Contemporary epistemology studies not only skeptical arguments, but also major responses to them.

The skeptic often says: “You cannot rule out error, illusion, dreaming, deception, or false assumptions. Therefore you do not really know.” Responses to skepticism ask whether this standard is reasonable.

Several common responses include:

  • Moorean Response: We know ordinary things, such as “Here is a hand,” more certainly than we know the skeptic’s argument is sound.
  • Contextualist Response: The standards for saying “I know” shift depending on context. Skeptical contexts raise the standards unusually high.
  • Pragmatic Response: Skeptical possibilities may be logically possible, but they do not govern ordinary rational life.
  • Transcendental Response: Skepticism depends on the very things it tries to doubt, such as reason, truth, and the laws of thought.
G. E. Moore (1873–1958): Taught at the University of Cambridge. Moore famously argued against skepticism by appealing to ordinary certainties, such as the existence of his hands.
Hilary Putnam (1926–2016): Taught at Harvard University. Putnam developed influential arguments against radical skeptical scenarios, including the “brain in a vat” problem.
Keith DeRose (1962–): Taught at Yale University. DeRose defended contextualism as a way of explaining why skepticism seems powerful in some contexts but not in ordinary life.

The strongest response for this map is the transcendental one: skepticism cannot even be formed unless reason, truth, and the laws of thought are already being used. The skeptic must stand on Reason in order to attack Reason.

Contemporary Epistemology
Closure Principle

The closure principle says that if you know one proposition, and you know that it logically entails another proposition, then you are in a position to know the second proposition.

In simple form:

If S knows P, and S knows that P implies Q, then S can know Q.

For example, if I know that Socrates is a man, and I know that all men are mortal, then I can infer that Socrates is mortal.

Closure matters because skeptical arguments often depend on it. The skeptic says: if you know you are sitting in a classroom, then you know you are not merely dreaming that you are sitting in a classroom. But you do not know that you are not dreaming. Therefore, you do not know you are sitting in a classroom.

Robert Nozick (1938–2002): Taught at Harvard University. Nozick famously denied closure in his tracking theory of knowledge.
Fred Dretske (1932–2013): Taught at the University of Wisconsin–Madison, Stanford University, and Duke University. Dretske argued that knowledge may not be closed under known implication in certain cases.

The debate over closure raises a deep question: does knowledge move through valid inference? If knowledge cannot be extended by implication, then reasoning itself is weakened. But if closure is accepted, skeptical arguments become more difficult to resist.

This shows why epistemology must study inference carefully. Logic tells us what follows from what, but epistemology asks when following the argument also gives knowledge.

Contemporary Epistemology
A Priori vs A Posteriori Knowledge

This distinction answers the question: Do we know by reason alone, or by experience?

A priori knowledge is known independently of experience. It is discovered through reason.

  • “All bachelors are unmarried.”
  • “2 + 2 = 4.”

A posteriori knowledge is known through experience.

  • “The sky is blue.”
  • “Water boils at 100°C at sea level.”
Immanuel Kant (1724–1804): Taught at the University of Königsberg. He argued that knowledge involves both reason and experience.
David Hume (1711–1776): Lived chiefly in Edinburgh and worked as a historian, librarian, and diplomat; he was not a university professor. He emphasized experience and questioned necessary connections.

The key issue is this: experience gives us particular facts, but not necessity.

For example, seeing the sun rise every day does not prove that it must rise tomorrow. That would require necessity, not merely observation.

So we must ask: where does necessity come from? It does not come from changing experience.

This leads us forward: knowledge requires something beyond changing experience—something stable and universal.

Contemporary Epistemology
Contextualism

Contextualism is the view that the standards for saying “I know” can change depending on the conversational or practical context. In ordinary life, the standards may be lower. In skeptical or high-stakes contexts, the standards may rise.

For example, in an ordinary conversation a person may say, “I know the bank is open on Saturday.” But if a large payment depends on it, or someone raises the possibility that the bank recently changed its hours, the standards for claiming knowledge become higher.

Contextualism tries to explain why skepticism can feel powerful in a philosophy classroom while ordinary knowledge claims still seem legitimate in daily life. It says that the word “knows” is sensitive to context.

Keith DeRose (1962–): Taught at Yale University. DeRose is one of the best-known defenders of epistemic contextualism.
David Lewis (1941–2001): Taught at Princeton University. Lewis developed an influential contextualist account in which knowledge depends on eliminating relevant alternatives.

The strength of contextualism is that it explains why ordinary knowledge and skeptical pressure both seem real. Its weakness is that it can appear to shift the meaning of knowledge rather than solve the problem of knowledge.

If knowledge changes with context, we must still ask: what is knowledge itself? Does context change what we know, or only what we are willing to say we know?

This points us back to the deeper question: knowledge must be more than social permission to speak confidently. It must be a true belief grounded in adequate evidence.

Contemporary Epistemology
Analytic vs Synthetic Judgments

The distinction between analytic and synthetic judgments asks whether a proposition is true by definition or true because of how reality is.

Analytic judgments are true because the predicate is already contained in the subject. For example: “All bachelors are unmarried.” To understand bachelor is already to understand unmarried man.

Synthetic judgments add something to the concept of the subject. For example: “The bachelor is six feet tall.” The concept bachelor does not already contain being six feet tall. We must learn that through experience or further information.

Immanuel Kant (1724–1804): Taught at the University of Königsberg. Kant made the analytic/synthetic distinction central to his account of knowledge. He famously argued for synthetic a priori judgments.
W. V. O. Quine (1908–2000): Taught at Harvard University. Quine challenged the sharp distinction between analytic and synthetic truths in “Two Dogmas of Empiricism.”

This distinction is important because it helps us ask what kind of truth we are dealing with. Is the proposition true because of the meaning of the terms, or because of the way things actually are?

It also connects back to concepts. If we understand the essence of a thing, then some propositions may follow from the concept itself. But if a predicate is not part of the essence, then we need additional evidence.

Contemporary Epistemology
Epistemic Normativity

Epistemic normativity asks: what ought we believe? It studies the duties, virtues, and standards that govern belief.

We do not merely happen to believe. We are responsible for many of our beliefs. We can be careless, gullible, prejudiced, dishonest, or lazy in the way we form beliefs. We can also be careful, honest, patient, humble, and courageous in the pursuit of truth.

Epistemic norms include:

  • Believe what is true.
  • Do not believe what is false.
  • Proportion belief to evidence.
  • Do not suppress known truth.
  • Revise beliefs when legitimate defeaters arise.
William K. Clifford (1845–1879): Taught at University College London. Clifford argued that it is wrong always, everywhere, and for anyone to believe on insufficient evidence.
William James (1842–1910): Taught at Harvard University. James replied that in some forced, live, and momentous options, the will may properly play a role in belief.
Linda Zagzebski (1946–): Taught at the University of Oklahoma. Zagzebski developed virtue epistemology, emphasizing intellectual character and the virtues of the knower.

This topic is especially important because it joins epistemology to ethics. The question is not only “What do I know?” but also “Am I loving truth rightly?”

If there are duties of belief, then the mind is under a moral law. That points beyond mere psychology to the question of the good, the true, and the God who grounds them.

Humean Problem
Problem of Induction

The problem of induction asks how we can know that the future will resemble the past. We often reason from repeated experience: the sun has risen every day, fire has burned in the past, and bread has nourished us before. But how do we know these patterns must continue?

David Hume made this problem famous. He argued that past experience does not logically prove future regularity. If we say that the future will resemble the past because it always has before, we are using induction to justify induction. That is circular.

David Hume (1711–1776): Lived chiefly in Edinburgh and worked as a historian, librarian, and diplomat; he was not a university professor. Hume argued that induction rests on custom or habit rather than demonstrative reason.
Karl Popper (1902–1994): Taught at the London School of Economics. Popper argued that science does not finally verify theories by induction; instead, scientific theories should be testable and falsifiable.

The problem matters because science, ordinary life, and planning all depend on stable patterns in reality. If induction cannot be grounded, then empirical knowledge becomes unstable.

This pushes us toward a deeper question: what makes nature orderly, intelligible, and dependable? If knowledge depends on stable order, then we must ask whether changing experience can ground that order, or whether knowledge ultimately points beyond change to something necessary and eternal.

Norms of Believing
Evidentialism

Evidentialism is the view that belief should be proportioned to evidence. A person ought not believe more than the evidence warrants, and a person ought not refuse belief when the evidence is sufficient.

This norm is important because belief aims at truth. If the mind ignores evidence, exaggerates evidence, or suppresses evidence, then belief becomes governed by desire, fear, pride, or social pressure rather than truth.

William K. Clifford (1845–1879): Taught at University College London. Clifford argued that it is morally wrong to believe on insufficient evidence.
Richard Feldman (1947–): Taught at the University of Rochester. Feldman is a major contemporary defender of evidentialism in epistemology.

The deeper question is not whether evidence matters, but what counts as adequate evidence and whether the knower is willing to follow it.

Norms of Believing
Doxastic Voluntarism

Doxastic voluntarism asks whether beliefs are voluntary acts of the will, or whether they are judgments compelled by what appears true. This matters for the ethics of belief, responsibility for unbelief, Romans 1 suppression, and whether it makes sense simply to tell someone, “Just believe.”

Doxastic voluntarism is the view that belief is under the control of the will. Strong doxastic voluntarism says that we can directly choose beliefs. On this view, a person can decide to believe in something in the same direct way that he decides to raise his hand.

Weak doxastic voluntarism says that we cannot usually choose beliefs directly, but we can choose actions that shape belief: what we study, what evidence we consider, whom we trust, what habits we form, and whether we suppress or pursue truth.

This distinction helps explain why belief is not usually a bare act of choice. You cannot simply decide to believe that two plus two equals five while clearly seeing that it equals four. The mind is made to affirm what appears true.

Yet the will still matters. A person can refuse to look at evidence, avoid hard questions, seek flattering teachers, cultivate suspicion, love darkness rather than light, or suppress what is plain. In this way, unbelief can be morally responsible even if belief is not always directly chosen.

William James (1842–1910): Taught at Harvard University. James argued that the will may properly play a role in certain forced, live, and momentous options.
Bernard Williams (1929–2003): Taught at University College London, the University of Cambridge, and the University of California, Berkeley. Williams argued against direct voluntary control over belief.

The key issue is this: if belief is governed by truth, then the will should serve the mind’s pursuit of truth rather than replace it. The will can direct attention, inquiry, and honesty, but it cannot make contradictions true.

Norms of Believing
Intellectual Virtues

Intellectual virtues are habits of mind that help a person know truth. They include humility, honesty, courage, patience, carefulness, teachability, and love of truth.

A person may have intelligence and still lack intellectual virtue. Pride can make a person refuse correction. Cowardice can make a person avoid unpopular truths. Impatience can make a person accept the first easy answer. Dishonesty can make a person use arguments as weapons rather than as paths to truth.

Aristotle (384–322 BC): Taught at the Lyceum in Athens. Aristotle connected intellectual excellence to the proper formation of the rational soul.
Linda Zagzebski (1946–): Taught at the University of Oklahoma. Zagzebski helped develop virtue epistemology by applying virtue theory to knowledge.

Intellectual virtues remind us that knowing is not merely mechanical. The knower’s character matters because the love of truth can be strengthened or corrupted.

Norms of Believing
Epistemic Duties

Epistemic duties are duties we have as knowers. They include duties to seek truth, attend to evidence, reason carefully, revise beliefs when defeated, and avoid knowingly spreading falsehood.

These duties show that belief is not morally neutral. A person can be culpable for negligence, laziness, distortion, suppression, or refusal to consider what should be considered.

  • Seek: pursue truth rather than comfort.
  • Test: examine claims by Reason and evidence.
  • Revise: change beliefs when legitimate defeaters arise.
  • Witness truthfully: do not mislead others.
  • Do not suppress: do not bury what is clear because it is unwelcome.

Epistemic duties connect knowledge to moral law. The question is not only whether the mind can know, but whether the person is willing to know rightly.

Norms of Believing
Epistemic Sin / Vice

Epistemic sin or vice names corrupt habits of knowing. These include willful ignorance, self-deception, pride, intellectual laziness, cowardice, resentment of truth, and suppression of what is known.

These vices are not merely mistakes. A mistake happens when the mind errs despite trying to know. Vice appears when the person does not want truth, avoids truth, twists truth, or suppresses truth because truth threatens desire, status, sin, or ideology.

This category is especially important for Romans 1. Paul does not describe unbelief as innocent lack of data, but as suppression of the truth in unrighteousness. The problem is not simply that the mind lacks information; the problem is that the will and affections resist what is clear.

Epistemic vice shows why epistemology cannot be separated from ethics. To know rightly, the person must not only reason correctly, but also love truth more than self-protection, approval, or rebellion.

Alternative Account
Emotivism

Emotivism explains some claims, especially moral and religious claims, as expressions of feeling rather than as propositions that are true or false. On this view, when a person says, “This is good,” he may be doing something more like expressing approval than stating a truth about reality.

The epistemological problem is that if knowledge is reduced to emotion, then knowledge is no longer a true proposition supported by adequate evidence. Feelings can be powerful, but a feeling by itself does not prove that what is felt is true.

Emotivism therefore helps us see why knowledge cannot be merely expressive. To know, the mind must affirm what is true, not merely display what it feels.

Alternative Account
Voluntarism

Voluntarism emphasizes the will. In epistemology, a voluntarist account treats belief, assent, or commitment as rooted chiefly in the act of willing. A person may be said to “choose” belief or to commit himself to a claim by decision.

The danger is that knowledge becomes an act of will rather than a judgment governed by truth. But willing that something be true does not make it true, and choosing to believe something is not the same as having adequate evidence for it.

The will matters because we may love, hate, suppress, or pursue truth. But the will does not replace reason, truth, and evidence.

Alternative Account
Externalism

Externalism says that a person may know something even if he cannot internally give the proof for it, so long as the belief is produced by a reliable process or properly connected to the truth. The focus shifts from what the knower can demonstrate to the conditions outside the knower that make the belief reliable.

This can explain why children, ordinary observers, or skilled practitioners may know many things without being able to state a full argument. But it also raises a question: how do we know that the external process is reliable?

Externalism therefore presses us to think carefully about the difference between having knowledge and being able to explain how that knowledge is justified.

Main Path — Stage 3
Reason
The Three Laws of Thought

The turning point

Reason cannot be treated as one optional source among others. It is the power by which we form questions, understand answers, test contradictions, and move from knowledge to what is eternal.

When we define something we explain what sets it apart from all else. We do this by giving the laws that govern what that thing is. For example, if you wanted to define physical motion, you would give the laws of motion. If a thing is in motion it is obeying these laws. If something isn't obeying these laws it isn't in motion.

In the same way, we can define thinking by giving the laws of thought. If a person is thinking, these laws are being followed, and if these laws are being broken, then what is happening isn't thinking.

When we think we are using the laws of identity (A is A; you are you; God is God; good is good), non-contradiction (not both A and non-A; something is not both you and non-you, both God and non-God, or both good and non-good, at the same time and in the same respect), and excluded middle (either A or non-A; everything that exists is either you or non-you, God or non-God, good or non-good).

Can Reason Know Reality?

If Reason is the power by which we form concepts, judge propositions, and draw inferences, we must ask whether Reason reaches what is real. These branches consider self-knowledge and Augustine's account of divine illumination.

Logic
Logic (Aristotle)

Text Box

Aristotelian logic centers on terms, categorical propositions, and syllogisms. It studies how a predicate is affirmed or denied of a subject and how two premises can yield a necessary conclusion. Aristotle's logic is closely connected to demonstration: reasoning from what is known to what follows from it.

Aristotle's system is also tied to metaphysics. His categories, such as substance, quantity, quality, relation, place, time, and action, show that logic is not merely verbal manipulation. Thought is ordered toward being.

Compared with Ramus: Aristotle emphasizes syllogistic demonstration and the relation between thought and reality. Ramus later simplifies and reorganizes logic for pedagogy, method, and visual division.

Logic
Logic (Stoic)

Text Box

Stoic logic focuses less on categorical terms and more on whole propositions and the relations between them. It studies conditional arguments, disjunctions, conjunctions, and inference patterns such as “If P, then Q; P; therefore Q.”

This makes Stoic logic an important ancient predecessor to propositional logic. Where Aristotle often asks how subject and predicate terms relate, the Stoics ask how complete propositions imply or exclude one another.

Compared with Aristotle: Aristotelian logic is term-centered and syllogistic. Stoic logic is proposition-centered and conditional.

Logic
Logic (Ramus)

Text Box

Ramist logic, associated with Peter Ramus, reorganized logic around invention and judgment. Ramus wanted logic to be useful, teachable, and visually clear. He emphasized method, definitions, divisions, and branching diagrams.

1. Separation of Logic and Rhetoric. Aristotle integrated invention and arrangement into both logic for truth and rhetoric for persuasion. Ramus opposed this “confusion.” He assigned invention, the finding of arguments, and judgment, their arrangement, to logic, leaving style and delivery to rhetoric.

2. Elimination of Metaphysics. Aristotle bound logic to metaphysical categories such as substance and quantity, believing that the structure of thought reflects the structure of reality. Ramus stripped logic of much of this metaphysical and semantic baggage to make it more humanistic and practical. He argued that logic should reflect natural reasoning rather than abstract ontological structures.

3. Methodology and Visual Organization. Aristotle emphasized the syllogism as the primary engine of logic. Ramus prioritized axioms and a single method for all arts. He used dichotomous branching diagrams to divide subjects into opposing pairs, making complex information easier to visualize and memorize.

4. Pedagogical Focus. Aristotle aimed at establishing certain, scientific knowledge through formal demonstration. Ramus focused on usefulness. He replaced many intricate formal proofs with examples from classical literature, such as Cicero and Virgil, to show how logic is used in speeches and poetry.

Logic
Baconian Induction

Text Box

Baconian induction, associated with Francis Bacon, emphasizes disciplined observation, experiment, and the gradual discovery of causes. Bacon criticized hasty generalization and warned against idols of the mind: false patterns, verbal confusions, inherited prejudices, and theatrical systems of thought.

Baconian logic is not primarily syllogistic. It asks how the mind can move from observed cases toward reliable general knowledge of nature. It is therefore especially important for the rise of modern experimental science.

Compared with Aristotle: Aristotle emphasizes demonstration from first principles and syllogistic form. Bacon emphasizes discovery through methodical induction and experimental correction.

Logic
Boolean Logic

Text Box

Boolean logic, associated with George Boole, represents logical relations algebraically. It treats classes and truth values in symbolic form, using operations comparable to addition, multiplication, and negation.

This development made logic more mathematical and helped prepare the way for computer science, digital circuits, and formal symbolic systems.

Compared with Aristotelian logic: Aristotelian logic uses ordinary-language categorical forms such as “All S is P.” Boolean logic abstracts from ordinary grammar and represents logical relations symbolically.

Logic
Fregean Logic

Text Box

Fregean logic, associated with Gottlob Frege, created modern predicate logic. It analyzes propositions with quantifiers, variables, functions, predicates, and relations. This allows logic to represent arguments that classical syllogistic logic cannot easily express.

Frege distinguished sense and reference and sought to show how arithmetic could be grounded in logic. His work became foundational for analytic philosophy and modern symbolic logic.

Compared with Aristotle: Aristotle's logic analyzes categorical propositions using subject and predicate terms. Fregean logic analyzes quantified structures and relations with far greater formal precision.

Logic
Russell & Whitehead / Modern Logic

Text Box

Bertrand Russell and Alfred North Whitehead developed modern symbolic logic in Principia Mathematica. Their project, often called logicism, attempted to show that mathematics could be grounded in logic. Modern logic translates propositions into symbolic form using variables, predicates, relations, and quantifiers.

One major issue is the meaning of existence in categorical propositions. In modern logic, “some” is read existentially: “Some S is P” means “there exists at least one S such that S is P.” This is sometimes called existential import. It matters because it changes the way we read the traditional Square of Opposition.

In older Aristotelian logic, universal propositions were often treated as if the subject term referred to something real. Therefore, “All S is P” could imply “Some S is P.” But in modern logic, “All S is P” can be treated as true even when no S exists. For example, “All unicorns are one-horned animals” may be counted true because there are no unicorns that violate the claim, while “Some unicorns are one-horned animals” is false because no unicorns exist.

This shows a major difference between classical Aristotelian logic and modern symbolic logic. Aristotle’s logic is more closely tied to real predication about beings. Modern logic often treats propositions as formal structures whose truth conditions can be evaluated without assuming that the subject exists.

Compared with Frege: Frege created the modern predicate logic that made this kind of quantifier analysis possible. Russell and Whitehead extended the symbolic project into a systematic attempt to rebuild mathematics on logical foundations.

Logic
Modal Logic

Text Box

Modal logic studies necessity and possibility. Instead of asking only whether a proposition is true or false, it asks whether it is necessarily true, possibly true, impossible, or contingent.

Necessary: what cannot be otherwise. If a proposition is necessarily true, then it is true in every possible world. For example, “A is A” is not merely true here and now; it must be true wherever thought is possible.

Possible: what could be true without contradiction. Something is possible if its denial is not required by reason and if the thing described can coherently exist.

Impossible: what cannot be true because it violates reason or entails contradiction. A square circle is impossible because it would require something to be both square and not-square, circular and not-circular, in the same respect.

Contingent: what is true but could have been otherwise. A contingent thing exists, but it does not exist by the necessity of its own nature. It depends on something else.

Common Modal Logic Systems

Most normal modal logics are built on top of a foundational system called K, named after Saul Kripke. By adding different axioms or rules to K, logicians create stronger systems.

System K: the weakest base system. It assumes only the most basic rules for necessity and possibility.

System T or M: adds the axiom that if something is necessarily true, then it is true: □A → A.

System S4: adds the rule that if something is necessary, then it is necessarily necessary: □A → □□A. This system is often used to model knowledge: if you know something, you know that you know it.

System S5: the strongest common modal system. It adds the rule that if something is possible, then it is necessarily possible: ◇A → □◇A. This is often used as the standard logic for metaphysical necessity.

System D: often used for deontic logic, the logic of morality, obligation, and permission. It assumes that if something is obligatory, then it is at least permitted. It rejects the idea that a person can be obligated to do the impossible.

System B: named after L. E. J. Brouwer. It adds the rule that if something is true, then it is necessarily possible: A → □◇A.

Why the Strange Names?

The names are mostly historical rather than descriptive. K is named after Saul Kripke, who transformed modal logic with possible-worlds semantics. S1–S5 come from C. I. Lewis, one of the founders of modern modal logic, who numbered his systems from 1 to 5. T comes from Kurt Gödel’s use of that letter for a modal axiom system. B is named in honor of L. E. J. Brouwer, though Brouwer did not invent the system.

Different Interpretations of Modal Logic

The mathematical structure of modal logic can be interpreted in different ways, depending on how we understand the symbols □ and ◇.

Alethic Logic: the logic of necessity and possibility. This is the standard use of modal logic when we say, “it must be true” or “it could be true.”

Epistemic Logic: the logic of knowledge and belief. Here necessity can be interpreted as what is known or fixed within a knower’s information.

Deontic Logic: the logic of obligation and permission. Here modal terms are interpreted morally: what must be done, what may be done, and what must not be done.

Temporal Logic: the logic of time. Modal operators can be used to express claims such as “it will always be the case” or “it was once the case.”

Plantinga and the Necessary Being

Alvin Plantinga used possible-worlds modal logic to formulate a modal ontological argument. The central idea is that if a maximally great being is possible, then such a being exists in some possible world. But maximal greatness includes necessary existence. Therefore, if a maximally great being exists in any possible world, that being exists in every possible world, including the actual world. In simpler terms: a necessary being, if possible, is not merely possibly necessary; it is necessary.

This is why modal logic matters for theology and metaphysics. It lets us ask whether God is merely a very great contingent being, or whether God is necessary being itself. If God is necessary, then God does not merely happen to exist. God cannot not exist.

Classical Theism and Necessity: classical theism also speaks of God as necessary being, but usually grounds this in God’s aseity, simplicity, eternity, and independence. God exists by the necessity of His own nature and is the source of all contingent beings.

Compared with ordinary categorical logic: categorical logic asks how subject and predicate are related. Modal logic adds the mode of truth: whether something must be, may be, cannot be, or merely happens to be.

Logic
Deontic Logic

What Deontic Logic Studies

Deontic logic is the logic of duty, obligation, permission, and prohibition. It studies propositions that use moral or legal terms such as ought, may, must, and must not. In ordinary modal logic, the box symbol can mean “it is necessary that.” In deontic logic, the same kind of symbol is interpreted normatively: “it is obligatory that.”

For example, if O(A) means “A is obligatory,” and P(A) means “A is permitted,” then a basic deontic principle says that if A is obligatory, A must at least be permitted. This is one reason System D is often associated with deontic logic: it rejects the idea that a person can be obligated to do what is impossible or morally forbidden.

Main Contributors

Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646–1716) anticipated parts of modal and normative logic by analyzing necessity, possibility, and rational order. Ernst Mally (1879–1944) produced one of the earliest formal systems of deontic logic, though his system generated paradoxes. Georg Henrik von Wright (1916–2003) is usually treated as the founder of modern deontic logic because his 1951 essay “Deontic Logic” gave the field its modern form. Later contributors include Alan Ross Anderson (1925–1973), who connected obligation with sanctions, and Roderick Chisholm (1916–1999), whose work on contrary-to-duty obligations showed how difficult moral and legal reasoning becomes when someone has already violated a duty.

How It Differs from Other Modal Logics

Alethic modal logic asks what must be true or may be true. Epistemic logic asks what is known or possibly known. Deontic logic asks what ought to be done, what may be done, and what must not be done. The formal symbols can look similar, but the interpretation changes. Necessity becomes obligation; possibility becomes permission.

This makes deontic logic especially important for ethics, law, and political philosophy. Moral and legal systems often contain claims about what people must do, what they may do, and what they are forbidden to do. Deontic logic helps test whether those claims fit together or produce contradictions.

Deontic Paradoxes

Deontic logic also reveals problems. Suppose a person ought not steal. But if he steals, he ought to confess. Does that mean there is an obligation that depends on violating a prior obligation? These are called contrary-to-duty obligations. They show that moral reasoning cannot be reduced to a simple list of commands. We also need to understand order, hierarchy, permission, impossibility, excuse, and guilt.

A simple deontic principle is often stated as ought implies can: if someone is morally obligated to do something, then it must be possible for that person to do it. This is why deontic logic must distinguish impossible duties, conflicting duties, and duties that arise after wrongdoing.

Contrast: Epistemic S4 and “Knowing That You Know”

The material about the KK Thesis belongs most directly to epistemic logic, not deontic logic, but it is useful here because it shows how the same modal symbols change meaning under different interpretations. In epistemic logic, the S4 axiom is known as the KK Thesis, or Positive Introspection:

□A → □□A

When applied to knowledge, the box means “it is known that.” So the axiom says: if I know A, then I know that I know A. In symbolic form this is often written Kp → KKp.

The reliable mechanic: imagine you take your car to a mechanic. He tells you, “The alternator is dead.” At level one, you know the alternator is dead because a trusted expert told you (□A). Under S4 logic, you also know that you know the alternator is dead (□□A). You are aware of your state of knowledge, so you can plan your budget, call for a ride, and explain the situation to others.

Why this is controversial: S4 may be too strong for ordinary human knowers. You might know how to ride a bike without knowing that you know the physics of counter-steering. Under stress or intoxication, a person might know a fact, such as his home address, while losing the reflective ability to verify that he knows it.

S4 and S5: S4 says, “If I know it, I know that I know it.” S5 adds Negative Introspection: if I do not know something, then I know that I do not know it. S4 treats knowledge as transparent; S5 treats ignorance as transparent too. S4 may model a rational mind reflecting on its own knowledge, while S5 looks closer to an omniscient mind that is perfectly aware of the boundaries of its own ignorance.

The comparison helps clarify deontic logic. In epistemic logic, □ means “known.” In deontic logic, □ means “obligatory.” So □A → □□A would not mean “if A is obligatory, then it is obligatory that A is obligatory” unless we deliberately choose that deontic interpretation. The logic is formal, but the philosophical meaning depends on how the modal operator is interpreted.

Modal Logic
Gödel

Kurt Gödel

Kurt Gödel (1906–1978) was an Austrian-born logician and mathematician. He was born in Brünn, then part of Austria-Hungary, studied and worked in Vienna, and later moved to the United States, where he worked at the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton, New Jersey.

Gödel is best known for his incompleteness theorems. The first incompleteness theorem says that in any sufficiently powerful, consistent formal system capable of doing arithmetic, there will be true statements that cannot be proven within that system. The second incompleteness theorem says that such a system cannot prove its own consistency from within itself, assuming it really is consistent.

The importance of Gödel’s work is that it shows a limit within formal systems. Mathematics and logic cannot be reduced to a closed machine that proves every truth from inside itself. This does not destroy reason. Rather, it shows that formal proof systems depend on truths and assumptions that cannot all be generated from within the system itself.

Gödel also developed a modal ontological argument. Like later modal arguments, it uses the language of necessity and possibility to reason about God as a necessary being. This makes Gödel an important figure both for modern logic and for the philosophical use of modal logic in theology.

Epistemic Logic
Knowing You Know

The S4 Argument: Knowing That You Know

In epistemic logic, the logic of knowledge, modal systems can be used to model what it means to know. The S4 system is famous for the Positive Introspection Axiom, also called the KK Thesis. It explores the idea that knowledge is not merely possessing information, but also being aware that one possesses it.

The formula is Kp → KKp. That means: if a person knows p, then that person knows that he knows p. In ordinary modal notation, S4 is expressed as □A → □□A. When the box means “it is known that,” the axiom says: if I know A, then I know that I know A.

Detective example: Detective Miller knows that the suspect was at the scene (Kp). If S4 is correct, then if Miller knows the suspect was there, Miller also knows that he knows the suspect was there (Kp → KKp). Therefore, Miller can testify with certainty because he is aware of his own knowledge (KKp).

The reliable mechanic: you take your car to a mechanic and he says, “The alternator is dead.” At level one, you know the alternator is dead because a trusted expert told you (□A). Under S4 logic, you also know that you know the alternator is dead (□□A). You can now plan your budget, call for a ride, and explain the situation to others because you are aware of your own state of knowledge.

The subconscious contrast: in a weaker system such as K, the conclusion does not follow. A contestant on a game show might reflexively shout the correct answer before realizing he knew it. System K can allow that he possessed the knowledge (Kp) without knowing that he possessed it (not KKp). S4 resists this. In S4, knowledge is transparent: if the information is genuinely known, the knower is aware of knowing it.

S4 and S5: S4 says, “If I know it, I know that I know it.” S5 adds Negative Introspection: ¬Kp → K¬Kp. That means: if I do not know something, then I know that I do not know it. S4 treats knowledge as transparent; S5 treats ignorance as transparent too. For that reason, S5 can look less like ordinary human knowing and more like the knowledge of a mind perfectly aware of the limits of its own ignorance.

Why this is controversial: S4 may be too strong for human beings. A person might know how to ride a bike without knowing that he knows the physics of counter-steering. A person under stress or intoxication might know a fact, such as his home address, while temporarily losing the reflective ability to verify that he knows it. These cases suggest that human knowledge is not always perfectly self-transparent.

Plantinga and the question of knowledge: Alvin Plantinga’s work in epistemology emphasizes that a belief can have warrant when it is produced by properly functioning cognitive faculties in the right environment according to a design plan aimed at truth. This helps us ask whether knowing that we know requires reflective proof, or whether some knowledge is immediate and warranted before we can give a second-level account of it. Epistemic modal logic gives the formal structure for that question; Plantinga’s epistemology asks what makes the knowledge warranted in the first place.

Logic
Paraconsistent Logic

Text Box

Paraconsistent logic studies systems in which contradictions do not entail everything. In classical logic, from a contradiction anything follows; this is sometimes called explosion. Paraconsistent logicians reject explosion so that reasoning can continue in the presence of inconsistent information.

This kind of logic is often discussed in relation to inconsistent databases, legal reasoning, semantic paradoxes, and debates about whether some contradictions might be tolerable within a formal system.

Compared with classical logic: classical logic treats contradiction as destructive of rational inference. Paraconsistent logic tries to contain contradiction so that one inconsistency does not collapse the entire system.

Reason
Reason is Transcendental

We all begin with a starting point. The question is what is your starting point and is it the correct starting point. Different subjects might have different starting points relative to their subject. Ontologically, we can say all things start with God. But what about epistemologically?

When we say that Reason is Transcendental we mean that Reason cannot be questioned because it makes questioning possible. That doesn't say you shouldn't question Reason because it makes thought possible. It says you can't. You use Reason to form questions. And so you'd be using Reason to question if Reason can form questions. That's called a self-referential absurdity.

No other starting point is transcendental in that way. Someone might tell you to start with God or with God's Word, but that person used the laws of thought to form the thought “start with God.”

Another person might say, “If you don't start with God, you can't prove anything else.” That person used Reason to form that argument, and that argument is using the law of non-contradiction to say, “If you don't start with God, you end up contradicting yourself.” That means the argument is telling you to use Reason and avoid contradictions.

We always start with Reason if we are thinking. We cannot escape from Reason. We can only stop thinking and descend into meaninglessness.

Major Branch
Rationalism, Empiricism, and Kantian Synthesis

Definition

This branch asks about the source of knowledge. Does knowledge chiefly arise from reason, from sense experience, or from the mind's structuring of experience?

Rationalism

Rationalism emphasizes the role of reason, innate ideas, necessary truths, and deduction. René Descartes (1596–1650) lived chiefly in France, the Dutch Republic, and Sweden; Baruch Spinoza (1632–1677) lived and worked in the Dutch Republic; Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646–1716) lived and worked in German courts and intellectual circles. They were not primarily university professors. Rationalism looks for knowledge that cannot be reduced to changing sense impressions.

Empiricism

Empiricism emphasizes sense experience as the source of ideas and knowledge. John Locke (1632–1704) lived and worked in England and the Netherlands; George Berkeley (1685–1753) lived in Ireland and served as Bishop of Cloyne; David Hume (1711–1776) lived chiefly in Edinburgh and worked as a historian, librarian, and diplomat. Empiricism presses the question: what can be known from experience alone?

Kantian Synthesis

Immanuel Kant (1724–1804), who taught at the University of Königsberg, argued that knowledge requires both sensory content and the mind's forms and categories. He sought to answer Hume by showing that the mind actively structures experience. The cost is a distinction between things as they appear to us and things as they are in themselves.

Connection: This prepares students to see why the source of knowledge cannot be separated from questions about reason, experience, and reality.

Modern Challenge
Gettier Problem

Definition

The Gettier Problem challenges the claim that knowledge is simply justified true belief. Edmund Gettier (1927–2021), an American philosopher who taught at the University of Massachusetts Amherst, showed that a person can have a belief that is justified and true, yet still seems not to count as knowledge because the truth is reached accidentally or by luck.

Example

Suppose a person has strong evidence for a false belief, but by coincidence the proposition he believes turns out to be true for a different reason. The belief is true and justified in one sense, but the truth is not properly connected to the evidence.

Why It Matters

Gettier cases force epistemology to ask whether adequate evidence must be connected to truth in the right way. They expose the problem of epistemic luck.

Connection: This strengthens the knowledge page by showing that “true belief plus evidence” needs careful explanation.

Justification
Internalism and Externalism

Internalism

Internalism says that justification must be accessible to the knower's own mind. If you know, you should be able in some way to give or recognize the reason why your belief is justified. Roderick Chisholm (1916–1999), who taught at Brown University, is a major modern representative of internalist epistemology.

Externalism

Externalism says that a belief can be justified or count as knowledge because of a reliable truth-connected process, even if the knower cannot fully explain that process. Alvin Plantinga (1932–), who taught at Calvin University and the University of Notre Dame, developed an externalist account of warrant involving proper function.

Contrast

Internalism emphasizes what the mind can access; externalism emphasizes whether the belief is produced in the right truth-oriented way. The central question is whether justification must be visible from the inside or can depend on conditions outside awareness.

Structure of Knowledge
Foundationalism and Coherentism

Foundationalism

Foundationalism says knowledge rests on basic beliefs that support other beliefs. Descartes sought indubitable foundations in the thinking self. Classical forms of foundationalism look for first principles, self-evident truths, or properly basic beliefs.

Coherentism

Coherentism says a belief is justified by belonging to a mutually supporting system of beliefs. W. V. O. Quine (1908–2000), who taught at Harvard University, helped reshape modern views of knowledge as a web of belief rather than a structure resting on isolated foundations.

Problem

Foundationalism must explain why the foundations are known. Coherentism must explain why a coherent system is true rather than merely internally consistent.

Knowledge from Others
Testimony

Definition

Testimony is knowledge received from the word of another person. Much of what we know about history, science, ordinary life, and Scripture involves testimony.

Main Contributors

Thomas Reid (1710–1796), who taught at King's College Aberdeen and the University of Glasgow, argued that human beings are naturally formed to trust testimony unless defeated by contrary reasons. C. A. J. Coady (1936–), an Australian philosopher associated with the University of Melbourne, gave a major modern defense of testimony as a basic source of knowledge.

Question

The central question is not whether we use testimony—we constantly do—but when testimony is trustworthy, what defeats it, and whether all testimony must be reduced to sense experience or inference.

Intellectual Character
Virtue Epistemology

Definition

Virtue Epistemology studies knowledge in relation to the intellectual character, habits, and abilities of the knower. It asks whether knowing requires more than correct propositions: perhaps it also requires intellectual virtue.

Main Contributors

Ernest Sosa (1940–), who taught at Brown University and Rutgers University, developed an account of knowledge as apt belief, where the truth of the belief is due to intellectual competence. Linda Zagzebski (1946–), who taught at the University of Oklahoma, connected epistemology to virtue theory and the moral formation of the knower.

Connection

This branch connects epistemology to ethics: pride, laziness, cowardice, dishonesty, and love of error can damage the pursuit of truth.

Theology and Knowledge
Reformed Epistemology

Definition

Reformed Epistemology argues that belief in God can be rational, warranted, or properly basic without being inferred from an argument. It does not deny arguments for God; it denies that belief in God must always rest on an argument to be rational.

Main Contributors

Alvin Plantinga (1932–), who taught at Calvin University and the University of Notre Dame, developed the idea of warranted Christian belief and properly basic belief in God. Nicholas Wolterstorff (1932–), who taught at Calvin University and Yale University, contributed to Reformed epistemology, religious epistemology, and the critique of evidentialist assumptions.

Question

The central issue is whether knowledge of God must be mediated by proof, or whether God is known through properly functioning cognitive faculties in the right conditions.

Experience
Phenomenology

Definition

Phenomenology studies experience as it is given to consciousness. Rather than beginning with abstract theory, it attends to how things appear, how meaning is disclosed, and how the subject encounters the world.

Main Contributors

Edmund Husserl (1859–1938), who taught at the University of Halle, the University of Göttingen, and the University of Freiburg, founded modern phenomenology. Martin Heidegger (1889–1976), who taught at the University of Marburg and the University of Freiburg, transformed phenomenology toward questions of being, world, and existence.

Problem

Phenomenology can richly describe experience, but it must still answer how experience leads to truth about what is real beyond appearance.

Institutions and Knowledge
Social Epistemology

Definition

Social Epistemology studies the social conditions of knowledge: testimony, experts, institutions, disagreement, trust, authority, and the ways communities shape belief.

Main Contributors

Miranda Fricker (1966–), who taught at Birkbeck, University of London, New York University, and the City University of New York, is known for work on epistemic injustice: ways people can be wronged as knowers. Contemporary social epistemology also examines peer disagreement, academic authority, and institutional credibility.

Connection

This branch is especially important for evaluating universities, media, churches, courts, and scientific institutions. Social structures can serve truth, but they can also train people to suppress or distort what is clear.

Truth
Theories of Truth

Why Truth Matters

Knowledge requires truth. A person may sincerely believe a proposition and even have reasons for believing it, but if the proposition is false, it is not knowledge.

Correspondence Theory

On the correspondence theory, a proposition is true when it corresponds to reality. “S is P” is true if S really is P. This account fits naturally with the idea that the mind can know beings, properties, and relations as they are.

Coherence Theory

On the coherence theory, truth is understood by the way a belief fits within a larger system of mutually supporting beliefs. Coherence is important, because truth does not contradict truth, but coherence by itself cannot guarantee contact with reality.

Pragmatic Theory

On the pragmatic theory, truth is connected to what works, what proves useful, or what is verified in practice. This helps explain why truth matters for life, but usefulness is not the same as truth.

Main Figures

Aristotle (384–322 BC), who taught at the Lyceum in Athens, gave a classic correspondence formulation. Skepticism Node
Problem of Induction

Note: This is the conceptual node. It is most famously developed by David Hume in his skepticism about causation and induction.

Definition

The problem of induction asks how we can know that the future will resemble the past. We observe patterns: fire burns, bread nourishes, the sun rises, experiments repeat. But no number of past observations logically proves that the next case will be the same.

Hume's Challenge

David Hume (1711–1776), who lived chiefly in Edinburgh and was not a university professor, argued that induction cannot be justified by reason without circularity. To say “induction has worked before, so it will work again” already assumes the reliability of induction.

Why It Matters

The problem strikes at scientific reasoning, ordinary expectation, and practical planning. If nature is not ordered, and if the mind has no reason to expect stable relations, then empirical knowledge collapses into habit.

Push Toward Knowledge

The problem of induction pushes us beyond bare experience toward the need for an intelligible order in reality and a reliable rational power in the knower.

Reason and Reality
Divine Illumination

Definition

Divine illumination is the Augustinian account that the mind knows truth by the light of God. The created mind does not create truth; it is enabled to see truth because God is the eternal source of intelligibility.

Main Figure

Augustine of Hippo (354–430), bishop of Hippo in North Africa, argued that mutable human minds can know immutable truths only because truth is grounded in the eternal divine mind.

Epistemological Significance

This view connects Reason to God without reducing knowledge to private feeling. Reason is real, but its power to know eternal and necessary truth points beyond the changing human mind.

Reason and Reality
Self-Knowledge

Definition

Self-knowledge asks what the mind can know about itself: that it thinks, that it judges, that it doubts, that it wills, and that it is not identical to the objects it knows.

Main Figures

Augustine of Hippo (354–430), bishop of Hippo, used self-knowledge to show that the mind cannot doubt its own act of doubting. René Descartes (1596–1650), who lived chiefly in France, the Dutch Republic, and Sweden, argued that “I think, therefore I am” survives radical doubt.

Limit

Self-knowledge shows that thinking is undeniable, but it does not by itself explain all truth, the external world, or God. It is a starting point for reflection, not the whole of knowledge.

Moral Knowledge
How Do We Know the Good?

Natural Law

Natural law is the account that the moral order is knowable because human beings have a nature, and the good is what perfects that nature according to its proper end. Moral concepts are not mere preferences; they concern what a thing is and what fulfills it.

Main Figure

Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274), who taught at the University of Paris and in Dominican schools in Italy, argued that natural law is the rational creature's participation in the eternal law. Human beings can know basic moral principles through reason because the good is grounded in being and purpose.

Connection to Concepts

If we can form concepts such as human, good, evil, virtue, and law, then we can ask what belongs essentially to those concepts. Moral reasoning depends on forming true concepts and then judging propositions about what is good for human beings.

Main Path — Stage 6
Is Knowledge Possible Without Something Eternal?

The Question

Concepts require stable essences. Propositions require truth. Inferences require necessary relations. Induction requires an ordered world. Reason requires laws of thought that do not change from moment to moment.

The Problem

If all things are temporal, changing, contingent, and passing away, then what grounds necessary truth? What makes the laws of thought, the structure of reality, and the order of nature stable enough for knowledge?

The Next Step

This question moves the map from epistemology into metaphysics. We must ask what is eternal, and whether knowledge itself points us toward something eternal as the ground of truth and intelligibility.

Metaphysics
What is eternal?

The next question

Every definition of “God” depends on what is eternal. Is everything permanent? Is everything changing? Or is reality divided between what is permanent and what changes?

Choose the answer that best explains reality.

Option 1
All is permanent

The claim

This answer says that everything is permanent and that change is ultimately unreal or only apparent.

Problem

But we encounter change everywhere: motion, growth, decay, learning, willing, acting, and becoming. If all is permanent, then this changing world must be dismissed as illusion or appearance. The question then becomes: how can illusion, error, or appearance arise if nothing really changes?

Option 2
All is change

The claim

This answer says that everything is changing. There is no permanent being, no unchanging truth, and no eternal reality.

Problem

If all is change, then the proposition “all is change” would also change. It could not remain permanently true. Knowledge requires stable truths, stable meanings, and stable laws of thought. Total change destroys the conditions required to know that total change is true.

Option 3
Some things are permanent and some are changing

The claim

This answer says that reality includes both what is permanent and what changes. This preserves the evidence of change while also preserving the stable truths needed for knowledge.

Why this advances the main path

If some things are permanent and some are changing, then we can ask what belongs to each category. Are truth, the laws of thought, moral law, and God eternal? Are material things, human lives, and historical events changing?

2. What is infinite, eternal, and unchangeable is spirit, and all is spirit.

3. Both matter and spirit are infinite, eternal, and unchangeable.

4. Only God is infinite, eternal, and unchangeable.

If you know that you are finite, temporal, and changeable, then you know that neither material monism nor spiritual monism can be true.

Religious Experience

Definition

Religious experience explains knowledge of God, the holy, or ultimate reality by appeal to experience: inward feeling, awe, dependence, encounter, mystical awareness, or transformation.

Main Contributors

Friedrich Schleiermacher (1768–1834), who taught at the University of Berlin, emphasized the feeling of absolute dependence. Skepticism Node
Relativism

Definition

Relativism says truth depends on the individual, culture, historical location, or interpretive community rather than corresponding to reality.

Failure Mode

Relativism usually presents itself as universally true. But if relativism is only relative, it does not bind anyone; if it is universally true, then relativism refutes itself.

Protagoras (c. 490–420 BC): Lived and taught in ancient Greece as a sophist. He is associated with the claim that man is the measure of all things.
Richard Rorty (1931–2007): Taught at Princeton University, the University of Virginia, and Stanford University. He rejected truth as correspondence and emphasized vocabulary, practice, and conversation.

Push back toward Knowledge: To deny universal truth, relativism must make a truth claim. The denial depends on what it tries to escape.

Skepticism Node
Subjectivism

Definition

Subjectivism says truth is determined by the individual subject: what is true for me may not be true for you.

Failure Mode

Subjectivism cannot explain disagreement. If two opposite claims are both true because they are sincerely held, truth has collapsed into preference.

George Berkeley (1685–1753): Lived in Ireland and served as Bishop of Cloyne. His immaterialism is not simple subjectivism, but it forced modern philosophy to ask how perception relates to reality.
Friedrich Nietzsche (1844–1900): Taught at the University of Basel before leaving academic life. His perspectivism influenced later subjectivist and anti-realist accounts of truth and value.

Push back toward Knowledge: Subjective conviction may explain what someone feels, but it does not prove what is true.

Skepticism Node
Postmodern Skepticism

Definition

Postmodern skepticism treats knowledge claims as constructions shaped by power, language, institutions, or social location.

Failure Mode

If all truth claims are power moves, then the claim “all truth claims are power moves” is also a power move rather than knowledge.

Michel Foucault (1926–1984): Taught at the Collège de France. He studied the relation of knowledge, discourse, institutions, and power.
Jacques Derrida (1930–2004): Taught at the École Normale Supérieure and later held appointments in France and the United States. His deconstruction questioned stable meaning and presence.

Push back toward Knowledge: Critique itself depends on truth: it must be true that a discourse is oppressive, false, evasive, or unjust.

Skepticism Node
Scientific Positivism

Definition

Scientific positivism says genuine knowledge is limited to what can be verified, measured, or confirmed by empirical science.

Failure Mode

The claim “only scientific claims are meaningful or knowable” is not itself a scientific claim. It is a philosophical rule smuggled in as if it were science.

A. J. Ayer (1910–1989): Taught at University College London and Oxford University. He popularized logical positivism and the verification principle in English-speaking philosophy.
Moritz Schlick (1882–1936): Taught at the University of Vienna and led the Vienna Circle, which advanced logical empiricism.

Push back toward Knowledge: Science depends on logic, mathematics, causality, and the reliability of reason; those are not established by laboratory measurement alone.

Skepticism Node
Linguistic Skepticism

Definition

Linguistic skepticism says language cannot reliably reach reality, or that meaning is trapped within language-games, interpretation, or social use.

Failure Mode

It uses language to tell us language cannot communicate truth. That means it depends on what it denies.

Ludwig Wittgenstein (1889–1951): Taught at the University of Cambridge. His later work emphasized meaning as use within forms of life.
Jacques Derrida (1930–2004): Questioned stable meaning through différance and textual play, influencing linguistic and interpretive skepticism.

Push back toward Knowledge: Meaningful denial requires meaningful language. Language may be misused, but misuse is not the same as meaninglessness.

Skepticism Node
Moral Skepticism

Definition

Moral skepticism denies that we can know moral truths, or denies that moral claims are true or false at all.

Failure Mode

Moral skepticism is usually asserted with moral urgency: we ought not judge, ought tolerate, ought expose power, or ought reject oppression. But “ought” claims presuppose moral truth.

J. L. Mackie (1917–1981): Taught at the University of Oxford. He defended moral error theory, arguing that ordinary moral claims presuppose objective values that do not exist.
A. J. Ayer (1910–1989): His emotivism treated moral claims as expressions of approval or disapproval rather than truth-apt propositions.

Push back toward Knowledge: If moral skepticism is offered as something we should accept, then it has already appealed to a norm of belief.

Alternative Account
Expressivism

Definition

Expressivism says some statements, especially moral or evaluative statements, primarily express attitudes, commitments, or prescriptions rather than describe reality.

Failure Mode

If knowledge becomes expression, then disagreement becomes a clash of attitudes rather than a dispute about truth. But expressivism itself is offered as a true account of what our language is doing.

Charles L. Stevenson (1908–1979): Taught at Yale University and the University of Michigan. He developed emotivist and expressivist accounts of ethical language.
Simon Blackburn (1944–): Taught at the University of Cambridge and the University of North Carolina. He defended quasi-realism, an expressivist attempt to explain why moral discourse appears realist.

Push back toward Knowledge: Expression can accompany knowledge, but it cannot replace the truth of the proposition expressed.

Alternative Account
Non-Cognitivism

Definition

Non-cognitivism says certain claims, especially moral claims, do not state propositions that can be true or false. They express emotions, commands, prescriptions, or commitments.

Failure Mode

Non-cognitivism must give a proposition about the meaning of moral language. It therefore uses propositional knowledge to deny propositional status to other claims.

A. J. Ayer (1910–1989): Taught at University College London and Oxford University. He held that moral statements express feeling rather than factual knowledge.
R. M. Hare (1919–2002): Taught at Oxford University. His prescriptivism treated moral language as universalizable prescription rather than description.

Push back toward Knowledge: If a theory explains what moral language means, it gives a truth claim about language.

Alternative Account
Mysticism / Religious Experience

Definition

Mysticism appeals to immediate religious experience, union, illumination, or encounter as a way of knowing beyond ordinary propositions.

Failure Mode

Experiences differ and conflict. To explain, test, or teach the experience, the mystic must use propositions about what happened and what it means.

Plotinus (204/5–270 AD): Lived and taught in Rome. His Neoplatonism emphasized ascent to the One beyond ordinary discursive thought.
William James (1842–1910): Taught at Harvard University. He studied religious experience psychologically and pragmatically in The Varieties of Religious Experience.

Push back toward Knowledge: Experience may prompt inquiry, but it must be judged by truth.

Alternative Account
Existential “Lived Truth”

Definition

Existential accounts often emphasize lived commitment, authenticity, decision, anxiety, or existence rather than abstract propositional knowledge.

Failure Mode

Authenticity and commitment still require truth. A person can be sincerely committed to what is false, destructive, or self-deceived.

Søren Kierkegaard (1813–1855): Lived and wrote in Copenhagen; he was not a professor. He emphasized subjectivity, inwardness, and the leap of faith.
Jean-Paul Sartre (1905–1980): Lived and worked in Paris as a writer and public intellectual; he was not chiefly a professor. He emphasized freedom, choice, and authenticity.

Push back toward Knowledge: A life can be passionately lived and still be false. Commitment must be ordered to truth.

Alternative Account
Knowing How

Definition

Knowing how is skill or ability knowledge, such as knowing how to ride a bike, play an instrument, or repair an engine.

Failure Mode

Skill knowledge is real, but when asked to explain or teach the skill, we turn to propositions: what to do, why it works, and how it differs from failure.

Gilbert Ryle (1900–1976): Taught at Oxford University. In The Concept of Mind, he distinguished knowing how from knowing that.
Michael Polanyi (1891–1976): Worked in physical chemistry and philosophy at the University of Manchester. He emphasized tacit knowledge: we know more than we can tell.

Push back toward Knowledge: Knowing how does not eliminate knowing that; it depends on truth whenever the skill is explained, corrected, or taught.

Alternative Account
Proper Functionalism

Definition

Proper functionalism says a belief has warrant when it is produced by cognitive faculties functioning properly in the right environment according to a design plan aimed at truth.

Failure Mode

This account gives a powerful external condition for knowledge, but it still raises the question: how do we know the design plan, proper function, and truth-aimed environment?

Alvin Plantinga (1932–): Taught at Calvin University and the University of Notre Dame. He developed proper functionalism as an account of warrant and applied it to religious belief.

Push back toward Knowledge: Proper function helps explain how beliefs can be warranted, but the account still requires truth, reason, and propositions.

Alternative Account
Anti-Representationalism

Definition

Anti-representationalism rejects the idea that knowledge is a mental representation corresponding to reality. It often treats language as a tool for coping rather than mirroring.

Failure Mode

If the theory says representation is false, it has represented reality as being that way. It cannot escape truth claims about thought and language.

Richard Rorty (1931–2007): Taught at Princeton University, the University of Virginia, and Stanford University. He rejected the “mirror of nature” picture of knowledge and emphasized conversation and practice.

Push back toward Knowledge: Tools can be useful because they fit reality in some way. Usefulness does not replace truth.

Alternative Account
Pragmatism

Definition

Pragmatism emphasizes what works in practice, what resolves doubt, or what proves fruitful in experience.

Failure Mode

Usefulness is not the same as truth. A false belief may seem useful for a time, and a true belief may be costly or inconvenient.

William James (1842–1910): Taught at Harvard University. He defended a pragmatic account of truth connected to what proves itself in experience.
John Dewey (1859–1952): Taught at the University of Chicago and Columbia University. He treated inquiry as problem-solving within experience and social practice.

Push back toward Knowledge: To say a belief works, we must still ask: works for what end, according to what standard, and is that standard true?
